The Broken Tile

 

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

In Fraction of Shape, students enter the value of the numerator that represents the pieces colored in the visual model. The fraction animates to create the visual model used in the prompt. When correct the colored pieces fill in the shaded sections, when incorrect the mismatched pieces fall to the ground and block JiJi’s path. The use of unconventional partitions in this game (see above) and the accompanying animation provide an opportunity for students to visually identify different unit fractions to describe how the visual model can been partitioned equally.

Students will:

  • Construct and describe equivalent ways to name a fraction of a given shape.
  • Justify why their descriptions are correct and how the two fractions are equivalent.
  • Reason from the whole to the parts (1 cut into 4 equal parts) and from the part to the whole (four of this shape <¼> makes the whole.)

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles:

Project a puzzle from level 3 that uses an equilateral triangle partitioned into sixths (trapezoids) or eighths (triangles). Ask students to solve the given puzzle and describe how the animation defines the role of a denominator and numerator in this context. Have students use available materials (see lesson below) to find equivalent ways to describe and justify what fraction of the shape is colored.

Task 2: Tangram problem:

After making tangrams with the students (see appendix B), challenge students to identify what fraction of the original square each piece represents. Cooperative groups work well with this task. (Option: read The Warlord’s Puzzleby Virginia Pilegard.) Compare different strategies for identifying the unit fraction represented by each tangram piece and how to prove they are labelled correctly.

Instructional Goals:

Common Core Content Standards

Develop understanding of fractions as numbers

3.NF.A.1 Understand a fraction 1/b as the quantity formed by 1 part when a whole is partitioned into b equal parts; understand a fraction a/b as the quantity formed by a parts of size 1/b.

3.NF.A.3 Explain equivalence of fractions in special cases, and compare fractions by reasoning about their size.

3.NF.A.3.A Understand two fractions as equivalent (equal) if they are the same size, or the same point on a number line.

3.NF.A.3.B Recognize and generate simple equivalent fractions, e.g., 1/2 = 2/4, 4/6 = 2/3. Explain why the fractions are equivalent, e.g., by using a visual fraction model.

4.NF.A.1 Explain why a fraction a/b is equivalent to a fraction (n × a)/(n × b) by using visual fraction models, with attention to how the number and size of the parts differ even though the two fractions themselves are the same size. Use this principle to recognize and generate equivalent fractions.

Standards for Mathematical Practice:

Practice Standard 1: Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them.

  • Are these shapes all sevenths? Why or why not?
  • How can you use what you know (this triangle is ¼ of the square) to figure out what fraction of the whole these other pieces are?

Practice Standard 3: Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  • How can the parallelogram and the square both represent the same fraction of the square? Can you prove it?
  • Do you agree or disagree with Sam’s reason why these are eighths? Why or why not?

Practice Standard 4: Model with mathematics.

  • What tangram pieces are equivalent? How do you know? How can you prove they are equivalent?

Practice Standard 6: Attend to precision.

  • What tangram pieces are equivalent? How do you know? How can you prove they are equivalent?

Practice Standard 7: Look for and make use of structure.

  • How does knowing the value of the large triangles help you figure out the value of the smallest triangles?

Practice Standard 8: Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning.

  • If you only knew the value of one shape, which one would you choose to help you figure out the value of the rest of the shapes? What would the value of the starting shape be? Explain your thinking.

Fraction Schema:

Students develop a deeper understanding of unit fractions and equivalence using area models by working with a ‘whole’ that has been partitioned into areas of different, but related, sizes. They develop multiplicative reasoning to derive the fractional value of each shape by identifying equivalent relationships among different shapes, e.g. “If 2 medium triangles make a large triangle and 4 large triangles make the whole square, then it takes 8 medium triangles to make the whole square. That means the medium triangle must be ⅛ of the whole square.” Students construct an understanding of unit fractions and equivalence by composing and decomposing the ‘whole’ and it’s fractional parts. They compose the whole, e.g. “it takes four of this large triangles to make the whole, so it must be ¼ and since it takes 2 of this medium triangle to make a large, then it must be ⅛,l” and decompose it, “e.g. cutting the square into four equal triangles makes each of these triangles ¼.”

Materials

  • 9”x12” construction paper cut into 9” x 9” squares* (1-black and 1-colored sheet per student)
  • scissors
  • grid paper
  • Making a Tangram directions (teacher copy)
  • ST Math templates (optional)

*The black square (9”x 9”) remains uncut to help the students be able to reference the ‘whole’ square. For the colored sheet, it helps to give students sitting adjacent to each other or sharing a table different colors to make their tangrams. This helps keep the pieces of a whole together: red, green, blue, yellow (for example.)

Anticipate Student Responses: How might students express their thinking?

Task 1: ST Math Fraction of Shape puzzles

  1. Students readily identify the number of colored pieces in the puzzle and explain how the denominator (1/n) partitioned (cut) the whole triangle into n-equal pieces and the numerator colored that many pieces.
  2. Student visualizes alternative ways to partition the whole triangle and explains how she can visually combine the unit fractions into larger unit fractions. Student may use rough sketches to demonstrate alternative partitions
  3. Student uses attached templates and combines partitions into equivalent areas and colors in the fraction represented in the puzzle.

Task 2: Tangram Fractions

  1. Build It!: Student identifies the largest triangles as fourths (¼) by creating the ‘whole’ square using four triangles. Then he builds the whole square using eight medium triangles and identifies them as eighths. There are not enough of the smallest triangles (1/16ths) within a group to build the whole square.
  2. Reason Multiplicatively: Student takes one large triangle and iterates it four times mentally or physically without using 4 triangles. Once these triangles are labeled, student places medium triangle on top of large triangle and notices that this is half of the large triangle. She reasons that if it takes two medium triangles to make the large triangle (¼) then it should take eight to make the whole square and label it ⅛. This approach can be extended to the smallest triangles. The square and parallelogram pose a bigger challenge.
  3. Non-congruent Pieces: Students use the smallest two triangles to build the square and parallelogram to prove they have the same value as the medium triangle (⅛.)
  4. Folding Square/Parallelogram: Student folds the shapes in half and see that it creates the smallest triangle.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Student cuts the shapes imprecisely and then ‘trims’ each piece so that all the pieces no longer line up or are proportional.
  • Student thinks there is no way to figure out the size of square/parallelogram because they are not triangles, i.e. does not see how the parallelogram and square can be decomposed into the small trian gles or vice versa
Misconceptions:
  • Student identifies fractions based on the total number of parts in the whole regardless of the fact that they are not ‘equal parts.’ They may see them all as 1/7 because the square is cut into 7 parts.
  • Student ignore the ‘whole’ square and treat other pieces as the whole.

Launch:

Pose the tasks

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzle

Students identify the ‘whole’ and describe how it is partitioned. Students understand what the puzzle is asking them to solve, i.e. naming the colored fraction. Get a puzzle wrong and have students discuss why it did not work. Students should describe the partitions and name the smallest ‘unit’ fraction.

Puzzle Analysis

3rd Grade/Fraction Concepts/Module 2/Fraction of Shape/Level 3

Level 3: The equilateral triangle partitioned into 4ths (trapezoids) with 2 smaller triangles colored in can help students visually partition these unit fractions (¼) into smaller units (⅛) and be able to describe the fraction of the whole triangle colored in using an equivalent fraction. The prompt for this puzzle makes this necessary.

Level 3: The equilateral triangle partitioned into 6ths (trapezoids) with 2 or 4 trapezoids colored in can help students visually combine these unit fractions (⅙) into larger units (⅓) and be able to describe the fraction of the whole triangle colored in using an equivalent fraction.

Select and Sequence

What student strategies will support the lesson’s math goals?

The sequence below is one example. You’re students may not have used all the strategies described below. Your students will vary in the language they use.

What sequence of strategies do you intend to use?

  1. Compare a direct modelling strategy with a multiplicative reasoning strategy:
    1. Student modeled the whole square by combining like pieces, e.g. 4-fourths, 8-eighths, and 16-sixteenths.
    2. Student iterated the large triangle four times to make the whole. Then they iterated two medium triangles to make ¼ and reasoned 2 for every fourth is 8. That must be ⅛.
    3. Student used smallest triangle to find all fractions.
  2. Share a misconceptions: the square and parallelogram are unknowable because they are not triangles or cannot be made into triangles and ask students if they agree/disagree and why?
  3. Have students share different ways to record the relationships they have found in tangrams.
    1. Drawing 1 large triangle = 2 medium triangles. Showing how two triangles can fit inside one larger triangle.
    2. Writing equations that show equivalence: ¼ = 2/8 = 4/16 and noticing relationships among equivalent fractions.

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher's Role:

Based upon the goals for the lesson decide on how you want to highlight and connect the mathematics in both the ST Math puzzle and the mathematical task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and compare representations, record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the ‘Tangram’ problem.

Ask the questions which make sense to you and are connected to your students’ solution strategies and the instructional goals.

  • How did you figure out the value of each of the tangram pieces?
  • How do you know that the large triangle is ¼? Can you prove it to the class?
  • Can you prove that the square, parallelogram, and medium triangle all have the same fractional value even though they are not congruent shapes?
  • How can we represent the relationships among the tangram pieces and between the pieces and the whole square? 
  • How do the ST math puzzles relate to the Tangrams lesson?
  • Can you think about some real world examples where we can use what we learned in this lesson? 

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students develop strategies for identifying unit fractions when the ‘whole’ is partitioned into differently sized pieces by finding relationships among unit fractions and the whole.
  • Students reason about the value of a unit fraction by identifying relationships among known fractions of different values: if two a’s are equivalent to one b and b is ¼ the whole, then a must be ⅛ because it takes four b’s to make the whole and eight a’s.
  • Students develop strategies for identifying the fractional value of non-congruent shapes within the same ‘whole’ area.
  • Students represent equivalence among fourths, eighths, and sixteenths using area models and equations.

    • What is your level of understanding of your classmate’s strategy?

    • Would you change any of your work? Why?
    • How are the strategies similar and how are they different?
    • Do you agree or disagree with your classmate’s strategy? Why or why not?

Tangram Instructions

This wonder puzzle composed of 7 geometric pieces formed out of a square is a great way to teach students about unit fraction and equivalence. The directions below will help you guide students as they create their own set of Tangrams using a 9” x 12” piece of colored construction paper.

  1. Make the rectangle into a square. Fold the short side to the long side so that the fold bisects the angle.
  1. Cut the extra paper.
  1. Open the paper and cut along the crease:
  1. Take one of the triangles and fold it in half by joining two of the acute angles together. Cut along the crease:
  1. Take the other triangle and mark the midpoint of the hypotenuse by joining two of the acute angles together. Then fold the angle above this midpoint and crease the fold. Cut along the crease:
  1. Take the trapezoid and fold it in half by joining the acute angles. Cut along the crease:
  1. Take one of the right trapezoids and fold the acute angle back to the adjacent right angle (like folding the tip of the toe to the heel on a shoe.) Cut along the crease:
  1. Take the other right trapezoid and fold the obtuse angle down to the opposite right angle (like folding the laces to the heel of a shoe.) Cut along the crease.
