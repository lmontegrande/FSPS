Building Fraction Schemas

What are schemas?

Schemas are neural networks that form a cognitive framework for how we perceive and interact with the world around us. Students start school with their own informal mathematical schemas. Taking these schemas into account and providing rich problem solving tasks and classroom discussions help students apply and extend prior knowledge while confronting and resolving possible errors and misconceptions. The tasks in this course are designed to activate student schemas around whole numbers, partitioning and equal shares, and the meaning of the four operations to support students’ solid construction of fraction schemas. Below are some key aspects of these schemas students will be developing as you engage them in the rich problem-solving tasks found in this course.

Connecting to Existing Schemas

Whole Numbers and Units

Everything needed for a student’s initial study of fractions is already in place with whole numbers. At the core of this schema are the concepts of ‘Quantity,’ ‘Counting’ and ‘Number.‘ Long before they learn number names or symbols (Number) or how to use the counting sequence (Counting), students as infants have a sense of quantity. They have the concept of more/less and can subitize (visually recognize sets) up to 5 objects. Fractions are numbers and students need to see them as a natural extension of their number schema.
Through early elementary grades students develop a formal understanding of whole numbers based on working with 1. This unit, “1 whole,” is at the core of the whole number schema. As students learn to count, they generate the next number by counting ‘1’ more. All whole numbers can be modeled and understood as being composed of a set of ‘1 whole’ units. Early work with addition and subtraction involves counting on or counting back by ones.

The idea of a counting unit is expanded through place value experiences to apply to units other than one whole: “1-ten” or “1-hundred.” The whole number schema now extends to incorporate place value and an understanding of different ‘units:’ e.g., 345 = 300 + 40 + 5 = (3x100)+(4x10)+(5x1) = 3 hundreds + 4 tens + 5 ones.

Unit Fractions

Prior to formal introduction of fraction notation (e.g. 2/3), students should experience fractions as an extension of whole numbers by having to name unit fractions in contexts that describe ‘how much/many?’, e.g. I shared 5 apples with my brother and I get 2 and 1 half. This will support an interpretation of the denominator as naming the type of unit we are working with rather than as a separate quantity or number. Students begin to make the connections between the unit ‘whole’ and unit fractions, (e.g.,’1-fourth’). Unit fractions like ½ (1-half) or ¼ (1-fourth) behave the same way as whole number units, (e.g. 1-whole, 1-ten or 1-hundred). We can count them (e.g., ½ , 2/2, 3/2, 4/2), describe quantities in terms of them (e.g. I have 3 halves), and understand other fractions as composed of unit fractions (e.g. , 6/2 is 6-halves or later as 6/2 = ½ + ½ + ½ + ½ + ½ + ½.) Understanding fractions as numbers requires experiences that necessitate the need for new units, units between whole numbers. Fair sharing tasks that involve mixed numbers (e.g. 5 lemon bars shared among 2 friends) are an effective way to begin to build this understanding in a problem solving context.

Partitioning and Equal Shares

Students come to school with a foundation of sharing items fairly. This fair shares understanding progresses to partitioning unit wholes into equal shares. The problem type tasks in this manual provide an initial opportunity for students to partition and name the fair shares using unit fraction names. Building strong fraction schemas involves engaging students with these experiences prior to introducing formal notation and vocabulary of numerator/denominator or shading pre-partitioned shapes. Build on students’ informal language to connect to formal language and symbols. Labeling the unit fraction by naming the kind of unit prior to formal notation helps build this understanding, e.g. 1-third vs ⅓.

For example: 3 kids share 7 chocolates
S: Each kid gets 2 chocolates and a part of another.
T: How many of these parts would make a whole chocolate?
S: Three
T: We can all this part ‘a third’ because you need 3 to make a whole.

Equivalence, Comparison and Ordering

Textbooks tend to present students the same conventional partitions repeatedly during the course of the unit. It is important to engage students to actively partition shapes into many different areas. Extending students’ equal shares schema to include non-congruent areas that represent equal shares provides a foundation for understanding equivalence. In example A), while the fourths are non-congruent, they are equal shares of the same whole square. If these were candy bars of the same size, the amount shared for each person would be the same regardless of the shape of the one-fourth share.

In example B), partitioning the large rectangle into different sized areas provides opportunities for students to determine the unit value of each fractional area by thinking multiplicatively. Students begin to ‘see’ equivalence among the fractions, i.e. different ways to name the same area, by visualizing how 4 of the small yellow squares fit into the larger orange square. By iterating the partition up to the whole, e.g. it takes 4 of these pink rectangles to make the whole rectangle, students connect concepts learned earlier about naming unit fractions to working with equivalence.

These tasks also help students see that fractions define two ‘units’, the ‘unit whole’ and the ‘unit’ fraction. When the ‘unit whole’ is defined it can be partitioned into equal shares or equal intervals on a number line. One partition describes a ‘unit’ fraction. The number of ‘unit-fractions’ needed to compose a ‘unit-whole’ names the fraction, i.e. defines the denominator in the fraction.

Experiences with visual comparisons progress to symbolically comparing fractions with like denominators and focus on counting the number of equal partitions. Then move to comparing fractions with the same numerator. The size of the partitions have an inverse relationship to the number of equal partitions (e.g., 1/16 < ⅓). Students’ understanding of comparing and ordering fractions build upon their understanding of equivalence and the relationships between the numerator and denominator.

Number lines

An important aspect of the schema is understanding unit fractions represent numbers on a number line. In the number line model the location of unit fractions is determined by the distance of the interval from 0 to 1.

Models, such as number lines, support the construction of visual schemas which form a foundation for developing understanding about whole numbers, place value and the four arithmetic operations. The number line shows the iteration of a ‘unit’ to generate the next number in the sequence. All real numbers are relative amounts in comparison to the interval 0 to 1. Being able to ‘see’ consecutive whole numbers as equidistant points on a number line sets the stage for understanding that other numbers/points exist between whole numbers.

Number lines are an example of fractions as measures. The unit whole is defined as the distance from 0 to 1. The number line can then be successively partitioned into units of the same distance. All numbers on the number line are positioned relative to that distance. The distance between units can then be partitioned into any number of distances of equal measures.

Any distance on a number line can be divided into smaller equal partitions. Successive partitioning of any interval such as successive halving of the interval from ¼ to ½ will create infinite number of a/b fractions. This is an example of the density property of rational numbers.

Four Operations

The four operations expand and strengthen whole number schemas as students experience combining, separating, creating equal groups and partitioning objects into equal shares. Addition and subtraction, as joining or separating actions, build on counting schema and can be reinforced with number line models. Multiplication and division schemas build on grouping and sharing actions and can be reinforced with area models. Understanding whole number operations is extended to operations with rational numbers.

Multiplication and division play a particularly important role in supporting fraction schemas. These operations represent the heart of multiplicative reasoning, the ability to think relationally and proportionally through iterating units. Multiplicative reasoning is further developed when students understand a/b = a × 1/b or a/b = a ÷ b. Multiplicative reasoning is at the heart of understanding fractions and all rational numbers. Lesson 6, Alphabet City task involves understanding multiplicative relationships between part-part and part-whole. Students determine an area is ¼ of the whole area and realize that ¼ is partitioned into 2 areas and understand the area of each partition is ⅛ of the whole.

Engaging students in problem-solving tasks that activate these two schemas: 1-Number Schema: using a ‘unit’ to count and answer ‘how many’ questions, and 2-Operation Schema: operating on quantities to solve real world situations, support student understanding of fractions as a natural extension of their prior knowledge. Before introducing the symbols and vocabulary of numerator and denominators, students need to leverage their experiences with counting and fair sharing to build a formal understanding of their intuitive sense of halves, thirds, fourths, etc, and how they can be operated upon to solve real world problems.
