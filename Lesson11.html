Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Building a Number Line

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

Scale Fractions Addition and Subtraction asks students to compute the sum or difference of the given bar model and plot the answer on the number line. The bars move to the number line and show the action of adding or subtracting the second model to or from the first model. This game connects the bar model to a number line by defining the distance from zero to 1 (the unit whole) using a whole bar and demonstrating the action of adding or subtracting mixed numbers represented by the bars.

Students will:

Build bar models using connecting cubes, Cuisenaire Rods, or paper strips and use their bar models to build a number line. They will use their number line to model an addition and subtraction problem given in the Scale Fraction game.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles

Project a puzzle from levels 1 (addition) and 2 (subtraction). Students analyze Scale Fraction Addition and Subtraction. Students solve the given puzzles and explain how the animation defines the operations by placing bars on a number line. Ask students how they might use jumps to represent the action of the the bar model moving to the number line and animating the addition and subtraction.

Task 2: Building a Number Line

Project a puzzle from level 3, addition problem that requires regrouping of fourths. Ask students to create a bar model of the addition problem. How could this problem be modeled on the number line? Use your bar model to create a number line and represent the action of addition in this problem on your number line. Project a subtraction problem from level 4. Students represent this problem on the number line, using similar strategies as before.

Instructional Goals:

Common Core Content Standards

Develop understanding of fractions as numbers.

3.NF.A.2 Understand a fraction as a number on the number line; represent fractions on a number line diagram.

3.NF.A.2.a Represent a fraction 1/b on a number line diagram by defining the interval from 0 to 1 as the whole and partitioning it into b equal parts. Recognize that each part has size 1/b and that the endpoint of the part based at 0 locates the number 1/b on the number line.

3.NF.A.2.b Represent a fraction a/b on a number line diagram by marking off a lengths 1/b from 0. Recognize that the resulting interval has size a/b and that its endpoint locates the number a/b on the number line.

Build fractions from unit fractions by applying and extending previous understandings of operations on whole numbers.

4.NF.B.3.b Decompose a fraction into a sum of fractions with the same denominator in more than one way, recording each decomposition by an equation. Justify decompositions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model. Examples: 3/8 = 1/8 + 1/8 + 1/8 ; 3/8 = 1/8 + 2/8 ; 2 1/8 = 1 + 1 + 1/8 = 8/8 + 8/8 + 1/8.

4.NF.B.3.c Add and subtract mixed numbers with like denominators, e.g., by replacing each mixed number with an equivalent fraction, and/or by using properties of operations and the relationship between addition and subtraction.

Standards for Mathematical Practice:

Practice Standard 2:Quantitative Reasoning.

  • A student changed this mixed number to a fraction greater than 1 (e.g., 2 2/5 to 1 2/5) to solve the problem.
    • Explain why you would or would not use this strategy to help you solve the addition problem.

Practice Standard 3: Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  • Are both of these representations correct? How do you know?
  • Do you agree or disagree with this solution? Defend your claim.
  • Prove that this number is equal to this expression. (e.g., 2 2/5 = 1 + 7/5)

Practice Standard 4: Model with mathematics.

  • How can you represent the problem using an equation?
  • How do jumps on a number line represent addition? Subtraction?

Fraction Schema:

Extending whole number schemas into rational numbers includes experience with how operations behave with this new set of numbers and how these new numbers affect the operations. Creating bar models of mixed numbers and connecting them to number lines provides students with experiences that bridge whole number addition and subtraction. The model shows addition as joining and subtraction as taking away. These can be represented as jumps on the number line.

Launch:

Pose the tasks

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles

Students identify all relevant visuals and understand what the puzzle is asking them to solve. Get a puzzle or two wrong and have students discuss why it did not work. Students explain how addition and subtraction can be represented as adding to and taking away bars on a number line.

Puzzle Analysis

Grade 4/Adding and Subtracting Fractions/Scale Fraction Addition and Subtraction/levels 1, 2, 3, 4

Level 1: Puzzles show a bar model for adding two mixed numbers. The whole bars move to the number line and join end to end along the number line. The whole bars move first, then the fraction bars are added. Puzzles in this level use fraction sums ≤ 1 whole.

Level 2: Puzzles show a bar model for subtracting fractions/mixed numbers. The bars of the minuend move to the number line. The bars of the subtrahend move on top of the existing bars and take away the existing bars from the number line.

Level 3: Many puzzles now have fraction sums >1.

Level 4: Many puzzles have fractional parts in the subtrahend that are less than the fractional part in the minuend. A fraction of a whole bar is subtracted.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?

  • What do you see in this puzzle? (ask students to pair share at least 3 things they notice)
  • What happens to the size of the bar as it comes down to the number line? Does that change the value? (The bar shrinks. It shows one whole, but the whole is smaller to show the bar and number line model refer to the same ‘unit whole.’)
  • How does the animation show where the sums or differences should go on the number line?
  • How could you represent the action of adding (subtracting) this number on the number line with a jump in place of a bar?

Task 2: Pose the task.

  1. Pose the task - Create a model of the addition problem using any manipulative you choose. Why can any size number line be used to represent this problem? (Number lines generated from different sized wholes can be used to represent this problem. They just cannot be used together, e.g., ¼ on one plus ¼ on the other does not equal ½ if they refer to different wholes.)
  2. Use your bar model to create a number line and represent the addition problem on your number line. How can you represent the action of addition using jumps to represent the bars?
  3. Show a subtraction problem. Ask students to represent the subtraction problem on their number line using jumps to represent the bars. How can you represent the action of subtraction using jumps to represent the bars?

Encourage students to think of a plan to solve the mathematical task.

  • What strategy will help you solve this task? (SMP 1, 4)
  • What tools do you want to use? (SMP 5)
  • How will you record your thinking? (SMP 3,4,6)
  • How will you evaluate your solution? (SMP 1)

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students express their thinking?

Task 1: ST Math Scale Fractions puzzles

Students will readily see how the mixed numbers are decomposed into whole bars which move along the number line and fractions that are combined then added to the whole numbers on the number line.

  1. Students are able to use jumps from left to right to represent the action of the bars moving along the number line to represent addition. They use jumps from right to left to represent subtraction.

Task 2: Creating a bar model and number line

Students create their bar model of the problem and create a number line to represent the problems. Students represent the bars as jumps on the number line (left to right for addition and right to left for subtraction).

  1. Connecting cubes:Students may use 4 connecting cubes to measure out one whole, use 2 cubes to measure out the one-half and 1 cube to measure out one-fourth.
  2. Paper strips: Students may create fractions by folding paper strips. They use the one whole strip to measure out the distance of one on the number line, half of a strip for one-half and half of the half for one-fourth.
  3. Cuisenaire Rods: Students may use purple to measure out one whole, red for one-half and white for one-fourth.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Students make calculation errors.
  • Students miscount points on the number line.
Misconceptions:
  • Students add the whole numbers and add the numerators and denominators in the fractions.
  • Students add the whole numbers to the numerators in each mixed number.
  • Students add the whole numbers, add the fractions and combine these two numbers into a mixed number without decomposing the fraction into a whole and the remaining fraction. (e.g., 3 5/4)
  • Students line up all of their bars to mark the line without partitioning the wholes and incorrectly label the number line (e.g., three wholes then 5 fourths labeled 1, 2, 3, ¼, ¼, ¼, ¼, ¼)

What questions will help students focus on the problem?

  • What does this bar represent in the problem?
  • How does your bar model help you create an accurate number line?
  • Think about what JiJi showed us when the bars were taken away from the number line. How can that help you use the number line to subtract?
  • How can you use your number line to represent the addition and subtraction calculations?
  • What happens to the numbers on a number line when you move to the right/left on the number line?

What questions will help you uncover your students’ thinking?

  • Describe what you have done so far?
  • How can you convince me that your number line is a good representation of what happened in the problem?
  • What does this jump/bar on the number line represent?
  • How did you determine how to partition your number line?
  • Why did you decompose this mixed number as you did?
  • How could you convert this mixed number into an improper fraction? How many (fourths) would there be in this mixed number?
  • How can you use this strategy to help you solve the problem?
  • How does this strategy relate to decomposing the mixed number?

What questions will help advance student thinking?

  • Are there other combinations of jumps/ways to place the bars you could have made on your number line to represent the action in the problem?
  • How does your number line representation compare to your bar model (calculations)?
  • Change all of your mixed numbers to fractions and show the equations.
  • Compare this method with the number line representation? Which works best for you and why?
  • What would you do to represent 3⅛ - 1⅝ on your number line?

Select and Sequence:

Misconceptions

Ask students before the share out if they feel comfortable sharing their misconception; misconceptions may be anonymous.

  1. Show an example of a misconception such as students who used a manipulative for the fourths that was not one-fourth of their whole. Have students identify the misconceptions.
  2. Students add the whole numbers, add the fractions and combine these two numbers into a mixed number without decomposing the fraction into a whole and the remaining fraction (e.g., 3 5/4). Have this student represent the numbers on a number line and name the endpoint. Compare the two numbers.

Solutions (possible order for solutions strategies)

  1. Share student’s work that only uses the bar model to represent the addition and/or subtraction.
  2. Share a number line that represents the addition and/or subtraction using jumps. Discuss how this relates to the action of adding to and/or subtracting from with the bar model. Have one or two students share how they created their bar model and represented the problem on the number line.
    1. “I used the connecting cubes to make my model because I knew I could use four to make the whole and then one would be one-fourth. To make my number line. I put the bar model on the number line just like JiJi did. I put all of the whole bars on, then all of the fourths. The answer was where I landed. When I did the subtraction problem. I did the same thing JiJi did. I put the bars for the first number on the number line. Then I put the bars from the second number on top of them. I took one away for each of these. When I ran out of fraction pieces I split the whole bar into fractions and then took away as many as I had from the second number.”
    2. “I cut up paper strips to make my bar model because it is easy to fold the strip in half 2 times to make fourths. I put the first number on the number line and then the second number on the number line and counted up to where I landed. That was the answer. It didn’t matter how I put the bars on the number line. They still end up at the same place. For the subtraction problem, I put the strips for the first number on. I laid the strips for the second number on top of the first number and took away those strips that matched up. I didn’t have enough of the fourths, so I traded the whole for four fourths and took away as many as I had left from the second number.”
  3. Have a student share a number line that represents the addition and subtraction with jumps along the number line. Compare this with the bar models.
  4. Have a student share converting the mixed numbers into improper fractions and adding and/or subtracting the fractions. Discuss the numbers and how they relate to the number line and bar models.

Summary:

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher’s Role:

Highlight students’ strategies and reasoning that connect to the mathematics in the task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and discuss ways to compare representations, record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the “Building a Number Line” task that support learning goals.

  • How are the representations the same?
  • How are the representations the different?
  • Which jumps on this student’s number line represents this equation? What equation do these jumps represent?
  • Is this student’s work clear, without them explaining it to you? Explain.

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students make connections among the operations, bar models and the number line model. Connect back to the JiJi puzzles.
  • Students discuss what they need to make an accurate and consistent number line.
  • Students describe how to move on a number line to represent addition and subtraction.
  • Students are able to model a problem on paper so that others can follow their thinking.
  • Students understand why they need to decompose a mixed number to subtract when the fraction in the minuend is less than the fraction in the subtrahend and they justify their decomposition.
  • Students add and subtract mixed numbers by replacing each mixed number with an equivalent number.

Student’s Role:

  • Students will have an opportunity to use the Standards of Mathematical Practice listed in the Goals section.
  • Students will share their mathematical thinking with the class.
  • As your classmates share their thinking, you need to think about:
    • What is your level of understanding of your classmate’s strategy?
    • What questions can you ask to clarify your own understanding of the strategy?
    • How does your classmates’ strategies compare to your strategies?
    • Would you change any of your work? Why?
    • How are the strategies similar and how are they different?
    • Do you agree or disagree with your classmate’s strategy? Why or why not?
