Mandy's Special Dog Food

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

In Fruit Monster, feeding the monsters the correct amount of food makes them go away and lets JiJi cross. Each puzzle gives you how much one monster eats (unit rate) and either the number of monsters needing to be fed or the amount of fruit you have to feed the monsters. Changing what is known and unknown in the puzzles allows students to develop strategic reasoning and a schema for understanding the relationship between multiplication and division: Monsters × Fruit/Monster = Total Fruit.

Students will:

Develop strategies for solving equal group problems, first with whole numbers and then with fractions. The total amount of fruit to share equally with the monsters is based upon the specified rate of fruit per monster and number of monsters. They will connect the Fruit Monster game level 1 with a multiple groups/multiplication task.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzle

Project a puzzle from level 1 and have students discuss what the puzzle is asking them to do, and ask them to identify what is known and unknown in the problem. Have students solve one or two puzzles and share their solutions.

Task 2: Mandy's Dog Food

Pose the problem, a dog eats 1⁄ 2  cup of dog food a day for 8 days, monitor students as they develop strategies and select key strategies to discuss. Compare different strategies students used to solve this problem.

Fraction Schema:

Unit fractions ( 1⁄ b) play a key role in connecting fractions to whole numbers and providing a conceptual foundation for later work with rational numbers. In the dog food problem, unit fractions provide a basis for counting units of the size 1⁄ b, e.g. 1-half, 2-halves, 3-halves. This connects to the notation we use for fractions a/b--> 1⁄ 2  2⁄ 2  3⁄ 2  4⁄ 2  and supports understanding that fractions a⁄ b  are formed by "a" parts of the size " 1⁄ b." This iteration builds an understanding that a⁄ b  = a x 1⁄ b  which students will use in 4th grade. The task also provides an opportunity to understand that when a is a multiple of b, we can write that fraction as a whole number. They demonstrate the multiplicative relationship between the number of sharers (e.g. number of days to feed Mandy) and size of each share(e.g. serving size of dog food per day). If Mandy is fed 1⁄ 2  cup of her special dog food for 8 days, I will need 4 cups of Mandy’s special dog food. Students create a fraction ab ( 8⁄ 2) as the quantity formed by “a” (8) parts of size “ 1⁄ b” ( 1⁄ 2).

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students express their thinking?

Task 1: ST Math Fruit Monster puzzles

Students model the puzzle and solve the question, ‘How much fruit will we need to feed X monsters when each monster eats Y fruit?’

  1. Direct Modeling: Student uses cubes or drawings to represent each monster and how much fruit each monster needs. Then they count the total ‘fruit’ needed to feed the monsters.
  2. Counting: Student skip counts the fruit each monster eats as they keep track of the monsters in the puzzle, “3, 6, 9, 12. 4 monsters eating 3 fruit each need 12 fruit.”
  3. Derived Fact/Relational Thinking: Student looks at relationship between the number of monsters and the rate at which each one eats fruit, “4 monsters with 3 fruit each equals 12 fruit. 4 x 3 = 12.”

Task 2: Mandy’s Special Dog Food

Students develop strategies for counting out ½ cups of dog food for each of the 8 days. While there is only 1 solution for the problem, there are multiple strategies to find the solution.

  1. Students use visual models to show the known variables of the number of days and serving size of Mandy’s special dog food per day.
  2. They may use repeated addition to count the number of days of 1⁄ b  serving size per day. For example, they will add 1⁄ 2  8 times (e.g. 1⁄ 2 +  1⁄ 2 +  1⁄ 2 +  1⁄ 2 +  1⁄ 2 +  1⁄ 2 +  1⁄ 2 +  1⁄ 2 = 4 cups).
  3. They could use a ratio between the number of days and the serving size. One strategy is to double the number of days and the servings size.
  4. Multiply 8 days by 1⁄ 2  cup each day to equal 4 cups.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • representing either less or more than 8 servings (e.g. 1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  = 2 1⁄ 2 )
  • miscounting of ½ cups servings (e.g., 1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  +   1⁄ 2  = 2)
Misconceptions:
  • unable to identify the number of sharers/groups and the size of each share
  • misidentify the unit of the product (e.g. days instead of cups)
  • adding the number of days and the serving size (e.g. 8 + 1⁄ 2  = 8 1⁄ 2 )
  • adding numerator and denominator (e.g. 1⁄ 2  + 1⁄ 2  + 1⁄ 2  = 3⁄ 6)

Launch:

Pose the tasks

Task 1: Present ST Math Puzzles

Students learn how the puzzle works and describe what happens when the animation plays out. They explain why JiJi is not able to cross when you intentionally make a mistake. They identify the known variables (e.g. number of monsters and feeding rate/monster) and the unknown variable (e.g. total amount of fruit) in the puzzle. Several students share how they determined the solution for a puzzle.

Puzzle Analysis

3rd grade, Multiplication and Division Situations, Fruit Monster, level 1

Each puzzle in level 1 gives both a different rate of fruit per monster and number of monsters. The known variable is the number of sharers (e.g.monsters) and the size of each share (e.g. rate of fruit per monster) and the unknown variable is the total amount of items (e.g. fruit) to share. This is a multiple groups/multiplication problem type.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?
  • What do you notice in this puzzle? Ask students to pair share at least 3 things they notice.
  • How does the puzzle work? (e.g. the chosen amount of fruit falls into the mouths of the monster)
  • What happens if you choose too few or too many pieces of fruit to share?
  • What is known and unknown in this puzzle?
  • How could you represent this puzzle using an equation? 

Task 2: Special Dog Food (multiple groups/multiplication)

  1. Pose the task: Read the task with the entire class without putting in the number choices the first time. During the second reading include the number choices you want the students to use.
  2. Ask the students how this task is similar to the Fruit Monster game. Students identify what is known and unknown in the task.
  3. Explain to the students to do the next set of numbers when they are finished with the first set. Be prepared to provide more challenging number sets if necessary (e.g., (6, 2⁄ 3), (8, 3⁄ 4), (12, 3⁄ 10).
Encourage students to think of a plan to solve the mathematical task.

  • How can the Fruit Monster problem help you solve the ‘Mandy’ problem? (SMP 7, 8)
