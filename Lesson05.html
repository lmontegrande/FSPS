Feeding Brody

 

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

In Fruit Monster, the goal is to feed the correct number of monsters using exactly the right amount of fruit given the rate each monster eats fruit to help JiJi cross the grass. The multiplicative relationship between these three quantities helps build an understanding of the inverse relationship between multiplication and division. Some levels give the rate monsters eat fruit (4 apples per monster) and the total number of monsters to be fed (3 monsters,) and ask ‘How many apples do you need to feed all the monsters? (3 groups of 4 equals 12 or 3 x 4 = 12.) This equal groups problem represents one model of multiplication. However, given the same rate, if the known is the total fruit available and the unknown changes to the number of monsters to be fed we have a very different problem. This puzzle now asks, ‘If each monster eats 4 apples, how many monsters can you feed with 12 apples?’ What was a equal groups multiplication problem now can be seen as a measurement division problem, ‘How many groups can you make with 12 apples if each group has 4 apples?’ Using measurement division problems helps students build conceptual understanding of division with fractions.

Students will:

Students solve for the correct number of fruit monsters needed to share the total fruit given based upon the fruit per monster rate. They connect their strategies for solving this puzzle to a more complex measurement division task involving fraction divisors. Students compare different strategies for solving fraction division problems.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzle

Project a puzzle from levels 3. Ask students what they notice in each puzzle, specifically, what is known and unknown. Start a discussion about how JiJi will and will not cross the grass to the other side. Although this puzzle may be trivially easy, discuss different strategies for solving the problem.

Task 2: Feeding Brody problem

Pose the problem, My dog, Brody, eats 1⁄ 2  cup of dog food every day. I am leaving 3 cups of dog food for Brody while I am away on a trip. If I am away for 7 days, will Brody have enough dog food for the entire time? Why or why not? Compare and contrast the students’ different solution strategies (e.g. visual models, counting/combining, relational thinking including derived facts).

Fraction Schema:

Students identify the known, total amount and size of each group (e.g. number of fruit per monster rate, amount of total fruit) and unknown number of groups(e.g. number of fruit monsters to eat the fruit) in a measurement division problem type. They identify the two units in measurement division problems(e.g. serving size of dog food for each day and the number of days Brody can be fed). Students connect the multiplicative relationships with the division relationship (e.g. Brody eats ⅓ cups of dog food every day and in three days he eats a whole cup. 1⁄ 3  × 3 = 3⁄ 3  =1, 4 cups x 3 servings⁄ day  = 12 days or 4 ÷ 1⁄ 3 = 12). They identify the number of servings which fit into the whole. (e.g. 4 cups with 1⁄ 3  cup served each day will last for 12 days.)

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students express their thinking?

Task 1: ST Math Fruit Monster puzzles

Students model the puzzle and solve the question, ‘Given how much fruit each monster eats and the total amount of fruit available, how many monsters can you feed?’

  1. Direct Modeling: Student uses cubes or drawings to represent all the fruit in the problem then distributes the fruit to each monster. The distribution may be 1 cube at a time or more efficiently by realizing that the monsters can get more than 1 block.
  2. Counting: Student counts out the fruit each monster gets until the total number of blocks are used up. E.g. “9 strawberries and 3 fruit per monster: 1 monster is 3, 2 monsters is 6, and 3 monsters is 9” or they skip count: “3, 6, 9 and understands each ‘count’ to be a monster.”
  3. Derived Fact/Relational Thinking: Student looks at relationship between animals and total blocks, e.g. “3 frogs and 9 blocks. How many blocks for each frog? 3 times what is 9? 3 x 3 = 9. Each frog gets 3 blocks.”

Task 2: Feeding Brody

  1. Non-Anticipatory Sharing: Students use visual models to show the unknown number of groups when the size of each group and the total amount is known. The size of each group in the “Will Brody Have Enough Food?”problem is a fractional amount of a cup . Students count the number of thirds within four cups in their visual model to determine the number of days that Brody can be fed.
  2. Additive Coordination: They may use repeated addition to count the number of groups of ⅓ by adding 12 1⁄ 3’s. They may count three thirds in one cup of dog food then multiply 4 × 3 = 12.
  3. Relational Thinking: Students use 4 ÷ 1⁄ 3  to determine the number of days Brody can be fed.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • partition a whole into 2, 3, 4, 6, 8 or 10 equal parts incorrectly.
  • misidentify the unit of the quotient (e.g. cups/days) 
  • use multiplication incorrectly (e.g. 4 × 1⁄ 3  = 4 ÷ 3⁄ 4  × 3 = 12
Misconceptions:
  • identify the problem type as partitive division rather than measurement division
  • add the denominators when using a repeated addition strategy

Puzzle Analysis

3rd grade/ Multiplication-Division Situations/Fruit Monster/Level 3

Given the rate at which fruit monsters eat fruit, Levels 3 puzzles switch the unknown from the total fruit given number of monsters and the number of monsters given total fruit in the sky. Select a puzzle with the known variables being the size of each group (rate) and the total amount of fruit to be shared. The unknown variable is the number of monsters or sharers needed. This creates a measurement division problem with whole numbers. Children’s understanding of how to solve measurement division problems with whole numbers provides a foundation for solving problems with a fractional amount for the size of each group.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?

  • What do you notice in this puzzle? Ask students to pair share at least 3 things they notice.
  • How does the puzzle work? (e.g. the fruit falls into the mouths of the fruit monsters based on the feeding rate/monster)
  • What happens if you choose too few or too many fruit monsters?
  • What is known in this puzzle? (e.g. feeding rate/monster and total amount of fruit) What is unknown in this puzzle? (e.g. number of fruit monsters needed to eat all the fruit).

Questions for the "Will Brody Have Enough Food?" task.

Ask the questions which make sense to you and are connected to your students’ solution strategies.

  • Where are the _______ cups represented? Where are the daily servings of the dog food represented?
  • How do you know your solution is accurate? How do you know you labeled the unit for the solution accurately?
  • What action is in the “Will Brody Have Enough Food?” task?
  • Why did ________ draw ______ cups in his/her visual model? Why did he/she split the visual model into ________ parts?
  • How does this ________ (e.g. visual model, numbers) connect with the “Will Brody Have Enough Food?” task?
  • How is 3 × 1⁄ 3  = 3⁄ 3  represented in __________ repeated addition strategy 1⁄ 3  + 1⁄ 3  + 1⁄ 3  = 3⁄ 3? How can you prove 3⁄ 3  = 1?
  • Do you agree 4 × 3 = 12 describes the math in the task? Why do you agree or disagree? What is your mathematical evidence?
  • How is 4 ÷ 1⁄ 3  different from 4 × 3? How are 4 ÷ 1⁄ 3  and 4 × 3 similar?
  • How is the Fruit Monster (e.g. Select Elephants game) similar and different to Brody’s task?

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Both the fruit monster and Feeding Brody problems have a similar structure. You know the size of each group and how much you are going to share among the groups. You just don’t know how many groups you can make.
  • A drawing can be used to represent the rate. Copying that rate (iterating) until you have the total amount of dog food available, tells you how many groups (days) you can feed the dog.
  • Skip counting by the rate until you get to the total (cans left for Brody) determines how many days Brody can eat.
  • A missing factor or measurement division equation can be used to solve this problem.
