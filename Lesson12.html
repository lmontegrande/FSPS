Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Enough Ribbon?

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

Scale Fractions Addition and Subtraction L.I. asks us to compute the sum or difference of the given expression and place the yellow ball on the number line to indicate the answer. The numerals in the expression turn into a bar model, move to the number line and show the action of adding or subtracting. This game connects the bar model to a number line by defining the distance from zero to 1 (the unit whole) using a whole bar and demonstrating the action of adding or subtracting mixed numbers represented by the bars. The animation demonstrates a strategy for adding and subtracting mixed numbers by decomposing them into a whole number and fraction.

Students will:

Students build bar models. Students use their bar models to model a subtraction problem. They model the problem using jumps on a number line to represent the bar model. They will solve a multi-step problem involving addition and subtraction of fractions and represent their thinking on a number line.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzle

Project a puzzle from levels 1, addition and 2, subtraction. ask students to analyze the animation and notice how the numerals are represented as a bar model and how the bar model moves to the number line and animates the problem. Ask students to represent a puzzle and its solution. Project a puzzle from level 5, subtraction problem that requires decomposing a whole number into an equivalent fraction or a mixed number into an improper fraction. How could this problem be modeled on the number line using jumps?

Task 2: Enough Ribbon?

Pose the Enough Ribbon problem (Joan and Brett were decorating picture frames for a class store project. They needed 3¼ feet of ribbon to decorate all their frames. Joan had 2½ feet of ribbon but used ¾ of a foot of her ribbon for another project. Brett had 2¾ feet of ribbon but used 5/4 of a foot of his ribbon for another project. Do they have enough ribbon for their project? Justify your solution.) Monitor students as the develop strategies and select key strategies to discuss. Compare different strategies students used to solve this problem.

Instructional Goals:

Common Core Content Standards

Build fractions from unit fractions by applying and extending previous understandings of operations on whole numbers.

4.NF.B.3.bDecompose a fraction into a sum of fractions with the same denominator in more than one way, recording each decomposition by an equation. Justify decompositions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model. Examples: ⅜ = ⅛ + ⅛ + ⅛ ; 3/8 = ⅛ + 2/8 ; 2⅛ = 1 + 1 + ⅛ = 8/8 + 8/8 + ⅛.

4.NF.B.3.cAdd and subtract mixed numbers with like denominators, e.g., by replacing each mixed number with an equivalent fraction, and/or by using properties of operations and the relationship between addition and subtraction.

4.NF.B.3.dSolve word problems involving addition and subtraction of fractions referring to the same whole and having like denominators, e.g., by using visual fraction models and equations to represent the problem.

Standards for Mathematical Practice:

Practice Standard 2:Quantitative Reasoning

  • A student changes a mixed number into a fraction (e.g., 2⅖ to 12/5) to solve the problem. Explain why you would or would not use this strategy to help you solve the subtraction problem.

Practice Standard 3:Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  • Are both of these representations correct? How do you know?
  • Do you agree or disagree with this solution? Defend your claim.
  • Prove that this number is equal to this expression. (e.g., 2⅖ = 1 + 7/5)

Practice Standard 4:Model with mathematics.

  • How can you use drawings, connecting cubes, rods, or paper strips to represent the numerals shown?
  • How do jumps on a number line represent addition? Subtraction?

Fraction Schema:

Students understand addition and subtraction of fractions as joining and separating parts referring to the same whole. They recognize how to decompose a mixed number to replace it with an equivalent fraction to help them add and subtract fractions with like denominators. Students understand how to use a number line to represent the action of adding and subtracting. They use this understanding to help them solve problems involving addition and subtraction of fractions.

Puzzle Analysis

4th Grade/Adding and Subtracting Fractions LI/Scale Fraction Addition and Subtraction LI/Levels 1, 2, 5

Level 1:Use a puzzle with fractions that do not add up to another whole. Have students analyze the puzzle to see how the numerals change to bar models and move to the number line to model the addition.

Level 2:Use any puzzle. Have students analyze the puzzle to see how the numerals change to bar models and move to the number line to model the subtraction.

Level 5:Use a subtraction puzzle that requires decomposing. Have students demonstrate how they could model this problem on the number line using jumps? Play the action back in teacher mode. Notice how the bars move along the number line and are removed from the number line. Ask students to demonstrate how this action could be represented using jumps along the number line.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?

  • What do you see in this puzzle? (ask students to pair share at least 3 things they notice)
  • How does the animation show the act of placing the numbers on the number line and adding them together or taking one away from the other?
  • How could you represent the action of adding (subtracting) this number on the number line with a jump in place of a bar?
  • What happens to the size of the bar as it comes down to the number line? Does that change the value?
  • Why can a number line that uses 8 cubes to represent the whole and a number line that uses only 4 cubes to represent the whole both work for solving this problem?

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students approach the task?

Task 1: ST Math Scale Fractions puzzles

Students build a model of the addition problem shown in the puzzle. They create a number line using the model and represent an addition and subtraction problem on the number line using jumps to represent the action.

  1. Connecting cubes:Students may use 4 connecting cubes to measure out one whole, use 2 cubes to measure out the one-half and 1 cube to measure out one-fourth.
  2. Paper strips:Students may create fractions by folding paper strips. They use the one whole strip to measure out the distance of one on the number line, half of a strip for one-half and half of the half for one-fourth.
  3. Cuisenaire Rods:Students may use purple to measure out one whole, red for one-half and white for one-fourth.
  4. Bar Model:Students lay their bars on the number line to represent addition. They lay the minuend on the number line and remove bars the match the subtrahend.
  5. Jumps:Students use jumps to represent the action of adding and subtracting on the number line.

Task 2: Enough Ribbon?

Students use different strategies to represent the addition and subtraction in the problem. Look for multiple representations to share.

  1. Bar Models:Students use a bar model or draw bar models and manipulate these models to solve the problem.
  2. Equations:Students use equations decomposing the mixed numbers into whole numbers and fractions or convert all of the numbers into fractions and perform the operations.
  3. Number line:Students draw number lines and represent the operations using jumps on the number line. Watch for students who just plot the final answer on the number line without using it to show the action of adding and subtracting the fractions.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Students make calculation errors.
  • Students miscount points on the number line.
Misconceptions:
  • Students do not add or subtract the appropriate numbers from the problem.
  • Students just add the amount of ribbon each person had and do not take away the amount they used.

What questions will help students focus on the problem?

  • What does this number represent in the problem?
  • Think about what JiJi showed us when the numerals were represented by bars. How would JiJi represent Joan’s ribbon?
  • How can you use your number line to represent the addition and subtraction calculations?
  • What happens to the numbers on a number line when you move to the right/left on the number line?

What questions will help you uncover your students’ thinking?

  • Describe what you have done so far?
  • How can you convince me that your number line is a good representation of what happened in the problem?
  • What does this jump on the number line represent?
  • What did you do first in your solution? Why?
  • How did your determine how to partition your number line?
  • Why did you decompose this mixed number as you did?

What questions will help advance student thinking?

  • Are there other combinations of jumps you could have made on your number line to represent the action in the problem?
  • How does your number line representation compare to your bar model (calculations)?
  • Change all of your mixed numbers to fractions and show the equations.
  • Compare this method with the number line representation? Which works best for you and why?
  • About how many picture frames do you think Joan and Brett could decorate? Justify your reasoning.

Select and Sequence:

What student strategies will support the lesson’s math goals?

The sequence below is one example. You’re students may not have used all the strategies described below. Your students will vary in the language they use.

Misconceptions (ask students before the share out if they feel comfortable sharing their misconception; misconceptions may be anonymous)

  1. Show an example of a misconception such as students who added the amount of ribbon Joan and Brett had without taking away the amount they used. Have students identify the misconceptions.
  2. Share a number line with misconceptions (i.e., incorrect number of jumps). Have students identify the misconceptions.

Solutions (possible order for solutions strategies)

  1. Students create bar models or drawings to represent the ribbon and the operations. Discuss what each model represents in the problem.
  2. Students use equations decomposing the mixed numbers into whole numbers and fractions or convert all of the numbers into fractions and perform the operations. Discuss and compare the different ways students represent the problems using equations.
  3. Have one or two students share how they solved the problem and represented it on the number line. Discuss and compare the jumps on the number line to the bar models and equations.
    1. “I made one number line for Joan and put a point at 2½or 2 2/4to show how much ribbon she started with. Then I jumped 3 one-fourths to the left to take away the ¾ feet of ribbon she used. Then I did Brett’s ribbon on another number line. I put a point at 2¾ and jumped back 5 one fourths. Then I added where I landed on both lines 1¾ + 1 2/4 and got 3¼ which is just enough ribbon.”
    2. “I made my number line in fourths. Then I put how much ribbon Joan had on it 2 2/4 I jumped to the right 2¾ to add Brett’s ribbon to Joan’s. Then I jumped to the left 3 fourths for how much Joan used and 5 fourths for how much Brett used. I ended up at 3¼ which is how much ribbon they need.”

Summary:

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher’s Role:

Highlight students’ strategies and reasoning that connect to the mathematics in the task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and discuss ways to compare representations, record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the “Enough Ribbon” that support learning goals

  • How are the representations the same?
  • How are the representations the different?
  • Which jump on this student’s number line represents this equation? What equation does this jump represent?
  • Is this student’s work clear, without them explaining it to you? Explain.

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students make connections among the numerals and operations, bar/drawing models and the number line model. Connect back to the JiJi puzzles.
    • When I jump back one whole jump on the number line, that is like when a whole bar on the puzzle was lifted away.
    • I added all of the whole numbers first. That is like when all of the whole bars on the puzzle came down first and moved to the right on the number line. I showed this with jumps to the right on the number line.
  • Students describe the different strategies and how they compare.
  • Students understand how the number line jumps represent addition and subtraction.
  • Students understand how to model a problem on paper so that others can follow their thinking.

Student’s Role:

  • Use the Standards of Mathematical Practice listed in the Goals section.
  • Share their mathematical thinking with the class.
  • As your classmates share their thinking, you need to think about:
    • What is your level of understanding of your classmate’s strategy?
    • What questions can you ask to clarify your own understanding of the strategy?
    • How does your classmates’ strategies compare to your strategies?
    • Would you change any of your work? Why?
    • How are the strategies similar and how are they different?
    • Do you agree or disagree with your classmate’s strategy? Why or why not?
