Multiplication of Fractions on a Number Line

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

Unit Multiplications on a NL asks the player to place the blast pad on the number line so that when the fraction is multiplied by a whole number, the fraction is placed on the number line and then iterated the given whole number of times. When a whole number is multiplied by a fraction, the whole number is placed on the number line and then divided into the same number of sections as the denominator of the fraction. The numerator is the number of the sections taken.

Students will:

Students use a Fractions on a Numberline game mat to represent multiplication of a fraction by a whole number and a whole number by a fraction. They demonstrate that multiplication of fractions is commutative but the size and number of iterations along a number line changes according to the role the factors play (multiplier or multiplicand). Students relate multiplication of fractions to multiplication of whole numbers.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzle

Ask students to solve select puzzles and describe their strategies. Ask students to connect their strategy to the visual proof (animation) given in the game. Ask them to describe the role of the denominator and numerator in multiplying a fraction by a whole number and a whole number by a fraction.

Task 2: Modeling Multiplication Problem on a Number Line

Given a Unit Multiplications on a NL Level 2 puzzle, students create a model of the multiplication problem by creating a number line and representing the action by partitioning the number line and making jumps on the number line. On another model they represent the same problem but reverse the whole number and fraction (multiplier and multiplicand).

Instructional Goals:

Common Core Content Standards

4.NF Build fractions from unit fractions by applying and extending previous understandings of operations on whole numbers.

4.NF.B.4 Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication to multiply a fraction by a whole number.

4.NF.B.4a Understand a fraction a⁄ b  as a multiple of 1⁄ b. For example, use a visual fraction model to represent 5⁄ 4  as the product 5 × ( 1⁄ 4), recording the conclusion by the equation 5⁄ 4  = 5 × ( 1⁄ 4).

4.NF.B.4b Understand a multiple of a⁄ b  as a multiple of 1⁄ b, and use this understanding to multiply a fraction by a whole number. For example, use a visual fraction model to express 3 × ( 2⁄ 5) as 6 × ( 1⁄ 5), recognizing this product as 6⁄ 5. (In general, n × ( a⁄ b) = (n × a)⁄ b.)

5.NF Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication and division to multiply and divide fractions.

5.NF.B.4 Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication to multiply a fraction or whole number by a fraction.

5.NF.B.4a Interpret the product ( a⁄ b) × q as a parts of a partition of q into b equal parts; equivalently, as the result of a sequence of operations a × q ÷ b. For example, use a visual fraction model to show ( 2⁄ 3) × 4 = 8⁄ 3, and create a story context for this equation. Do the same with ( 2⁄ 3) × ( 4⁄ 5) = 8⁄ 15. (In general, ( a⁄ b) × ( c⁄ d) = ac⁄ bd .)

Fraction Schema:

An understanding of partitioning the unit whole on a number line and iterating unit fractions leads to connecting fractions with the four operations. Relating whole number multiplication to multiplication of a whole number by a fraction and visualizing the action on a number line leads to a deeper understanding of the role of the multiplier and multiplicand in a fraction multiplication problem. Modeling multiplication of a fraction by a whole number on a number line facilitates an understanding of why multiplication by a fraction involves the act of dividing ( 1⁄ 5  of 20 is 1⁄ 3  × 20 but actually involves dividing by 5 (into fifths)). Interpreting the product of a⁄ b  × q as a parts of a partition of q into b equal parts; equivalently, as the result of a sequence of operations a × q ÷ by b facilitates an understanding of the role of the multiplier and multiplicand in a fraction multiplication problem.

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students approach the task?

Task 1: ST Math Unit Multiplication on a Numberline puzzles

Students solve multiplication of fractions by whole number problems and notice how the role of the factors is represented on a number line.

  1. Students will explain the animation in the puzzles that demonstrates the role of each factor in the puzzles.
  2. Students may need to use what they know about whole number multiplication to help them understand multiplication of fractions.

Task 2: Representing fraction by whole number multiplication on a number line

Students develop a deeper understanding of fraction multiplication by representing the role of the factors as partitions and jumps on a number line.

  1. Students divide the number line and represent the multiplication problems by jumps on the number line. They will reverse the role the factors play and represent that multiplication problem on the number line.
  2. Students may need to represent the problem with equations, drawings or manipulatives in order to understand what each move on the number line represents.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Students make calculation errors.
  • Students miscount markings.
Misconceptions:
  • Students may multiply the whole number by the denominator instead of the numerator.
  • Students may confuse multiplication with division and” invert and multiply.”
  • Students may confuse the number of groups with the size of the groups.
  • For a⁄b  fractions, students may make unit sized jumps the whole number of times.

Puzzle Analysis

5th Grade/Fraction Multiplication/Unit Multiplication on a Number Line/Level 1, 2, 4 (4th Grade/Fraction Multiples/Fraction Multiplication on a Number Line)

Level 1: Puzzles in level 1 have a whole number times a unit fraction. They show 0-1 partitioned into the unit fractions and the unit fraction is iterated the whole number of times.

Level 2: Puzzles in level 2 have a whole number times an a⁄ b  fraction. They show 0-1 partitioned into the unit fractions and a number of the unit fractions is shown as a jump. The jump is iterated the whole number of times.

Level 4: Puzzles in level 4 have an a⁄ b  fraction times a whole number. The whole number is marked on the number line and partitioned into b number of equal sized partitions from 0 to the whole number. One of these partitions is shown as a jump. The jump is iterated a number of times.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?

  • How is the numerator of the fraction represented in the animation? Denominator? Whole number?
  • How does the animation show the act of placing the numbers on the number line and multiplying them?
  • How many iterations (jumps) are there?
  • What is the length of each iteration (jump)?
  • How does this puzzle relate to the puzzle in level 2?

Select and Sequence:

What student strategies will support the lesson’s math goals?

The sequence below is one example. You’re students may not have used all the strategies described below. Your students will vary in the language they use.

  1. Show an example of drawings, cubes, Cuisenaire Rods or strips used to model the puzzle. Discuss the similarities and differences of each model to a number line model.
  2. Share a number line with misconceptions (i.e., 1⁄ 2  × 5 labeled as 5 jumps of size 1⁄ 2 ). Have students identify the misconceptions.
  3. Have one or two students share how they represented the problems (e.g., 2 × 2⁄ 3  and 2⁄ 3  × 2) on their number line. Discuss the different actions of the two expressions resulting in the same product.
    1. “I started at 4, because it is the second number. I divided the number line into 3 sections because that is the denominator in the fraction, so it shows the size of the jumps we need to take. Then I counted 2 of those jumps⁄ partitions  because that is how many jumps⁄ partitions  we need. I ended up at 8⁄ 3.”
    2. “I divided my number line into thirds. I knew I need to make 4 jumps because 4 was the number of copies of 2⁄ 3  I needed to make. I knew that my jumps had to be 2⁄ 3  long. So I jumped two thirds four times and landed at 2 2⁄ 3.”

Summary:

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher’s Role:

Highlight students’ strategies and reasoning that connect to the mathematics in the task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and discuss ways to compare representations, record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the “Multiplication of Fractions on a NL” that support learning goals.

  • How are the representations the same?
  • How are the representations the different?
  • Which factor tells how many times the other factor will be iterated?
  • When a whole number is iterated less than one whole time, what do we know about the relationship of the whole number to the product?

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students understand how the role of the multiplier and multiplicand determines how we can represent the product in fraction/whole number multiplication (iterating the fraction n times -2 x 2⁄ 3  or dividing then multiplying by the whole number -2⁄ 3  x 2)
  • Students understand that in a multiplication expression involving a whole number and fraction i.e., 2⁄ 3  x 18 means that you have 2⁄ 3  of 18, which is less than the whole 18. It is only 2⁄ 3  of it.
  • Students understand the similarities (the multiplier tells how many times to iterate the multiplicand) and differences (the product of a fraction and whole number is less than the whole number if the fraction is less than one).
