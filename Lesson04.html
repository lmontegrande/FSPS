Fractions on a Number Line

Scale Fractions

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

Scale Fractions is a game that helps players connect a bar model to a number line model by defining the distance from 0 to 1 (the unit whole) using the length of the whole bar. The goal of the game is to place the yellow ball on the number line so that when all the bars are laid end to end they reach the yellow ball. The end of the last bar has a claw that grabs the yellow ball and moves the number line up so JiJi can cross. The ball drops to block JiJi if incorrectly placed. In levels 4 and 5, students need to select the appropriate number of partitions from 0 to 1 to indicate the fraction represented in the bars. The animation shows the inverse relationship between number of partitions and their relative value, the more partitions the smaller the interval.

Students will:

Build bar models using connecting cubes, Cuisenaire rods, or paper strips, and use their bar models to create a number line. Students need to decide how to build the whole given the kinds of fractions being modeled.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles

Project a puzzle from Level 1, 2, and 4 and have students discuss what the puzzle is asking them to do, and ask them to identify how the bar models are placed on the number line. Have students compare the puzzles in the different levels. Have students solve one or two puzzles and share their solutions.

Task 2: Create a Number Line

Pose problem, build a model of a given puzzle and create a number line using the model. Monitor students as they develop strategies and select key strategies to discuss. Compare different strategies students used to solve this problem.

Common Core Content Standards

3.NF Develop understanding of fractions as numbers.

3.NF.A.1 Understand a fraction 1⁄ b  as the quantity formed by 1 part when a whole is partitioned into b equal parts; understand a fraction a/b as the quantity formed by a parts of size 1⁄ b.

3.NF.A.2 Understand a fraction as a number on the number line; represent fractions on a number line diagram.

3.NF.A.2.a Represent a fraction 1/b on a number line diagram by defining the interval from 0 to 1 as the whole and partitioning it into b equal parts. Recognize that each part has size 1⁄ b  and that the endpoint of the part based at 0 locates the number 1⁄ b  on the number line.

3.NF.A.2.b Represent a fraction a/b on a number line diagram by marking off a lengths 1⁄ b  from 0. Recognize that the resulting interval has size a⁄ b  and that its endpoint locates the number a⁄ b  on the number line.

Examples: 3⁄ 8  = 1⁄ 8  + 1⁄ 8  + 1⁄ 8; 3⁄ 8  = 1⁄ 8  + 2⁄ 8; 2 1⁄ 8  = 1 + 1 + 1⁄ 8  = 8⁄ 8  + 8⁄ 8  + 1⁄ 8.

4.NF.B.4 Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication to multiply a fraction by a whole number.

Fraction Schema:

Fractions like whole numbers are a directed location on a number line. Developing initial concepts of fractions as numbers occurs when encountering fractions as units of measure or as intervals on a number line. Actively constructing a number line by creating a unit whole and equal shares, then iterating them beyond 1 whole, provides students with a better understanding of why points on a number line, and their distance from 0, represent numbers. Discussing points on the number line ( a⁄ b ) as being composed of unit fractions ( 1⁄ b ) provides students with opportunities to understand:

  1. A fraction a/b is a number of aunits of bsize, e.g. 3⁄ 4  is 3-fourths rather than two numbers (3 and 4) stacked together.
  2. A fraction a⁄ b  can be decomposed into a sum of fractions with the same denominator.
  3. The idea above (#2) leads to making connections to multiplication of a fraction by a whole number when visualizing the number of times a unit fraction is iterated to the a/b point on the number line, 3⁄ 4  = 3 x 1⁄ 4

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students approach the task?

Task 1: ST Math Scale Fractions puzzles

Students determine the value of a bar model and the point on the number line for that value.

  1. Students visually combine the whole bars, then place them on the number line, slide them along the number line to the right and then place the fraction pieces on the number line to the right of the whole number.
  2. Students mentally count the whole bars and fraction of the bar model and locate that number on the number line.

Task 2: Building a bar model to create a number line

  1. Connecting cubes: Students may use 4 connecting cubes to measure out one whole, use one-half that many cubes to measure out the one-half and one-half that many cubes to measure out one-fourth.
  2. Paper strips: Students may create fractions by folding paper strips. They use the one whole strip to measure out the distance of one on the number line, half of a strip for one-half and half of the half for one-fourth.
  3. Cuisenaire rods: Students use a rod to represent one whole, a rod half its length to represent 1⁄ 2  and a rod half of that rod to represent 1⁄ 4  (e.g., students may use purple to measure out one whole, red for one-half and white for one-fourth).

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Students just draw a number line without using their bar model or any other reference.
  • Students miscount markings.
  • Students are not precise with laying the bars next to each other and marking their number line.
Misconceptions:
  • Students begin their counting 1 at the 0 hash mark.
  • A student may use a different number of cubes or different size bars for each of their wholes or a bar for the halves that is not one half of their whole.
  • Students label the points on the line greater than one as the fractional size without the whole number. ( 1⁄ 2, 1, 1⁄ 2, 2, 1⁄ 2)
  • Students place all of their bars side by side and label each point separately. (1, 2, 1⁄ 2, 1⁄ 2)
  • Students think the fraction is the ‘half bar’ or space between the hash marks (e.g., on the number line 1⁄ 2  is not the half bar but rather the length of the bar from 0 to 1⁄ 2.)

Puzzle Analysis

3rd Grade/Fractions on a Number Line/Scale Fraction/ Levels 1, 2 and 4. (4th or 5th grade Scale Fraction game may also be used)

Level 1: Puzzles define the length of a unit whole on the number line using whole bar models with the same unit length. Puzzles connect the bar model and the number line model with whole numbers.

Level 2: Puzzles introduce fractions and mixed numbers with halves, thirds, and fourths and connect the bar model with the number line. The number line is ticked by the same unit fraction used to partition the bar. A label appears after plotting the point on the number line showing mixed number notation. The bar model lays across the number line. Whole bars cover whole number distances and fractions measure out the distance between whole numbers.

Level 4: Puzzles now require the user to partition the number line unit whole into equal lengths to match the bar model. Partitions within the unit whole are labeled with the corresponding denominator showing the role of denominator as a divisor. Students should notice that as the number of partitions increases (the denominator gets bigger), the size of each piece decreases. This inverse relationship is a key outcome for early fraction concepts. The unit whole is clearly defined with a blue rectangle. After the number of sections is selected, the blue rectangle (unit whole) slides along the rest of the number line and partitions it into fractions of that size. Students select the appropriate endpoint as they did in the previous puzzles.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?

  • What do you see in this puzzle? (Ask students to pair share at least 3 things they notice.)
  • How does the animation show... (e.g., for Levels 1-3 the act of placing the numbers on the number line to select a point and the bar and the point represent a number)?
  • How is the number line partitioned and labeled in Levels 1-3? Does this change in different puzzles? Why?
  • How would you compare the whole bar of the bar model to the number line? (The length/height of the bar model is equal to the distance from 0 to 1 or 1 to 2.)
  • How does the number that appears when you move the ball, relate to the number line? (Level 2)
  • How else might the bars be placed on the number line? Does this change the endpoint? Explain?
  • What happens to the size of the bar as it comes down to the number line? Does that change the value? (Note: This is a perspective issue. The bars in the sky are closer and therefore appear bigger. The whole bar represents the interval from 0 to 1, not a specific length of an object.)
  • Discuss with a partner what you notice as the cursor is moved in right to left and back between 0 and 1. Why do you think this happens? Where should we click and why? (Level 4 and 5)
  • What happens to the denominator and number of sections as we move the cursor to the left from 1 to 0 in Level 4?
  • What can you tell us about fourths, sixteenths, fifths, and twenty-eighths?
  • What do you expect to happen when we click on that denominator?

Summary:

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher’s Role:

Highlight the students’ strategies and reasoning and connect them to the mathematics in both the ST Math puzzle and the mathematical task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and compare representations, record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the "Creating a Number Line" task that support learning goals.

  • How are the representations (bar model and number line) the same?
  • How are the representations different?
  • Can both representations be used to represent the same number?
  • Compare several number lines.
  • Do the number lines have to look the same? Why or why not?
  • Show two number lines with the 0 to 1 interval obviously different. Is the number 1 on this number line different from the number 1 on this number line? Why is it possible to represent 1 on both number lines?
  • How can we tell if a number line is drawn correctly?
  • Why is it important to pay attention to the spaces between numbers on a number line?
  • What is another name for this point? (1 1⁄ 2  or 3⁄ 2 )
  • How is counting the unit fractions like counting whole numbers?
  • If you make jumps of 1, how many jumps would it take to get to 7? Write this as an addition/multiplication equation.
  • Explain how to find 5⁄ 4  (or 7⁄ 2) on your number line. How many fourths (or halves) did you count? How could you write that as an addition or multiplication equation? (e.g., 5⁄ 4  is 5 one-fourths from 0, 1⁄ 4  + 1⁄ 4  + 1⁄ 4  + 1⁄ 4  + 1⁄ 4  = 5⁄ 4, or 5 × 1⁄ 4  = 5⁄ 4).
  • Why can you lay your rods on the number line in any order and still land at the same point?

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students count beyond 1 along the number line by unit fractions, e.g. 1⁄ 4, 2⁄ 4, 3⁄ 4, 4⁄ 4, 5⁄ 4, 6⁄ 4…
  • When appropriate, students use addition or multiplication equations to discuss location of numbers on the number line (e.g., 5⁄4  is 5 one-fourths from 0, 1⁄4  + 1⁄4  + 1⁄4  + 1⁄4  + 1⁄4  = 5⁄4, or 5 × 1⁄4  = 5/4).
  • Students understand that two separate number lines could use bar models with different size wholes. You can show all real numbers on each one because the unit whole defines where other numbers need to be placed (e.g., 3⁄2  is the midpoint between 1 and 2).
  • Students may choose to start their number line at any number. However, they need to understand that when creating a number line with their connecting cubes, s, or paper strips they need to place their whole bar at 0 (or their selected number) on the left end and mark the right end 1 (or the distance of 1 from the left end of the bar). For example: a student starts their number line at 2, the left end of the whole bar is placed at the 2 hash mark and 3 is marked at the right end of the bar.
  • Students understand that when they divide a number line into halves each iterated half is one more than the one to the left of it and that one-half to the right of 2 is 21⁄2, etc.
  • Students understand that two different number lines do not have to have the same distance between 0 and 1. However, on one number line the distance from 0 to 1 must be the same as the distance from 5 to 6.
