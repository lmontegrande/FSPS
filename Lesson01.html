Fair Shares

Partitive Division Problems

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

Place JiJi's path at the height where the blocks will stack evenly. If the path is too high or low there will be gaps or blocks on top keeping JiJi from crossing. The game uses a partitive division context where the students are given the total number of items to share and the total number of groups. They need to find the size of each group.

Students will:

Solve partitive division problems, first with whole numbers then connect those strategies to working with fractions. Students identify what is known (total items to share and number of groups) and unknown (size of each group) in the problem, develop strategies for partitioning the “left over” wholes into equal partitions that match the number of groups, and make connections between a variety of solution strategies.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles.

Project a puzzle from level 1 and have students discuss what the puzzle is asking them to do. Have students solve one or two puzzles and share their solutions.

Task 2: Fair sharing problems.

Pose fair sharing problem, Lemon Bars shared equally among friends, and monitor students as they develop strategies. Select key strategies to discuss and compare different strategies students used to solve this problem.

Instructional Goals:

Fraction Schema:

Fractions extend and build upon whole number schemas. Multiplication and division situations support this connection by providing students with the opportunity to apply multiplicative reasoning in situations that require partitioning ‘whole’ quantities into unit fractions in order to answer the question ‘how many’ with precision. Engaging students in problem solving contexts helps build an understanding of fractions as numbers in ways that they can understand and make sense of.

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students express their thinking?

Task 1: ST Math Fair Share puzzles

Students model the puzzle and solve the question, ‘Where should the path be so it is at the height of the blocks when they are shared equally among each animal?’

  1. Direct Modeling: student uses cubes or drawings to represent all the blocks in the problem then distributes the blocks to each animal. the distribution may be 1 block at a time or more efficiently by realizing that the creatures can get more than 1 block.
  2. Counting: Student counts out the block each animal gets until the total number of blocks are used up. E.g. “9 blocks and 3 frogs: each frog gets 1, that’s 3; another block makes 6; another (3rd) makes 9. Each frog gets three blocks. Or student skip counts: 3, 6, 9 and understands each ‘count’ to be a block for all three frogs.”
  3. Derived Fact/Relational Thinking: Student looks at relationship between animals and total blocks, e.g. “3 frogs and 9 blocks. How many blocks for each frog? 3 times what is 9? 3 × 3 = 9. Each frog gets 3 blocks.”

Task 2: Fair Sharing Problem

Students develop strategies for sharing lemon bars among friends that involve partitioning whole bars into equal shares. While there is only 1 solution for the problem, there are multiple strategies to find the solution.

  1. Non-Anticipatory Sharing: Student represents each lemon bar to be shared and distributes it to each friend. When they don’t have enough lemon bars for each friend to get a ‘whole’ bar they partition the bar into equal shares, most likely in halves, without thinking about how many halves that would make or how they relate to the number of friends sharing the pieces.
  2. Additive Coordination: Student represents the bars needing to be ‘split’ and cuts them based on how many friends are sharing them. If two bars are shared among 4 friends, they may cut the first one into fourths and give each friend one piece, then do the same with the second bar.
  3. Relational Thinking: Student realizes the four kids sharing the two bars can be solved by giving two friends one lemon bar each (i.e. seeing a simpler ratio or using a common factor to make the problem easier.) Then each friend gets half a bar.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Students do not find the ‘fair’ share because they miscounted one or more of the quantities.
Misconceptions:
  • Students choose either too many or too few for each share because they may not understand what it means to give each friend a “fair share” or “how to break apart” the leftover lemon bars.
  • Students randomly partition items to be shared so there are enough to share without making ‘fair shares.’
  • Student represents 1 1⁄ 2 lemon bars as “ 1⁄ 2” and reads numeral as 1 and one-half.

Launch:

Pose the tasks

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles.

Students identify what the puzzle is asking them to solve, e.g., Place JiJi’s path so that when the blocks are evenly distributed among each animal there are no gaps or extra blocks. Get a puzzle wrong and have students discuss why it did not work. Ask students to solve a puzzle with 12 cubes and 3 animals.

Puzzle Analysis

3rd Grade/Division Concepts/Module 1/Fair Sharing/Level 3

Puzzles in level 3 vary in the number of blocks (total items) to be shared and the number of animals (groups) that share them. Select a quantity that students will not automatically solve just by looking at the puzzle, e.g. 5 blocks and 5 frogs. A puzzle with 12 blocks and 3 or 4 animals or 15 blocks and 5 animals (see above) will produce a variety of student strategies for partitioning the blocks and seeing where the path should go.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?
  • What do you see in this puzzle? (ask students to pair share at least 3 things they notice)
  • What is JiJi asking us to figure out?
  • Why can’t JiJi cross if the path is too high/low?
  • How did you figure out how many blocks each animal gets?

What questions will help students focus on the problem?

  • How many lemon bars (blocks) are there to share?
  • Will (student doing the sharing) get any of the lemon bars? (If you add an additional sharer the solutions could have messy fractions for third grade.)
  • How many people (animals) are sharing the 6 lemon bars (blocks)?
  • What does it mean to fair share lemon bars with your friends?
  • What can you do with these remaining bars?
  • How can you represent your thinking on your paper?

  • How do your pictures, numbers or equations relate to the task?
  • How did you determine everyone’s fair share?
  • How much did each person receive?
  • How did you share the left-overs?

What questions will help advance student thinking?

  • What if (student doing the sharing) got a fair share?
  • Describe the left-over piece/s each person received. How may you name the left-over piece/s?
  • How would adding ___ more friends change your solution? What if ____ friends did not want any (lemon bars)?

Select and Sequence:

What student strategies will support the lesson’s math goals?

The sequence below is one example. You’re students may not have used all the strategies described below. Your students will vary in the language they use.

What sequence of strategies do you intend to use?
  1. Compare 2-3 student strategies beginning with non-anticipatory sharing to relational thinking (e.g. less to more sophisticated.)
    1. Deals out a ‘whole’ lemon bar to each friend. When two are left, cuts the first one in half and because there are not enough ‘pieces’ to give each friend one, they cut them in half again. Repeats process again. Answers that each friend gets 1 lemon bar and two "pieces."
    2. Gives each friend a whole bar (mentally and sees two bars are left.) Cuts each remaining bar in half and realizes there are four ‘halves’ and that is enough for each friend. Answers each friend gets ‘1 and a half lemon bars.’
    3. Divides the friends and lemon bars in half: 2 friends get 3 bars, so each one gets a bar and a half. Shows it as 1 1⁄ 2 bars.

Summary:

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher’s Role:

Highlight students’ strategies and reasoning to connect the mathematics in the task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and compare representations, record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the ‘fair shares’ task.

Ask the questions which make sense to you and are connected to your students’ solution strategies and the instructional goals.

  • How does _____________’s visual model compare with _________________’s equation?
  • How does a making smaller groups (relational thinking) compare to the repeated halving strategy? Which strategy do you prefer? Why?

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students describe the different numbers of cubes that could be used for the whole as any number that can be divided by two, because they needed to make a half.
  • Students understand that two students could represent the bar model with different numbers of cubes but one student could not use a different number of cubes to represent the wholes in their number.

Student’s Role:

  • Use the Standards of Mathematical Practice listed in the Goals section.
  • Share their mathematical thinking with the class.
  • As your classmates share their thinking, you need to think about:
    • What is your level of understanding of your classmate’s strategy?
    • What questions can you ask to clarify your own understanding of the strategy?
    • How does your classmates’ strategies compare to your strategies?
    • Would you change any of your work? Why?
    • How are the strategies similar and how are they different?
    • Do you agree or disagree with your classmate’s strategy? Why or why not?
