How Many Ways?

 

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

Select the rectangle that has been partitioned into equal areas and each piece will pull down JiJi’s path evenly. If the pieces do not have equal areas, the path will come down unevenly and JiJi will not be able to cross. Equal Areas partitions rectangles in different ways to help students visualize ‘equal shares’ and distinguish them from those that have not been partitioned equally.

Students will:

Students partition congruent squares into fourths in as many unique ways as possible in order to create ‘new puzzles’ for the game Equal Areas. They prove that all equivalent partitions within and across the squares have an equal area even though they do not ‘look the same’. Students must justify that nongruent partitions have equivalent areas.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles

Project a puzzle from level 2 and have students discuss what the puzzle is asking them to do. Ask students to describe how squares are partitioned differently and what happens when the partitions do not have equal areas.

Task 2: How Many Ways?

Compare two puzzles that partition a square into fourths differently. Ask students to use the tools at their disposal (e.g. grid paper, cut paper squares) to find as many unique ways to partition a square into fourths as they can. Compare different shaped fourths and prove why they represent 1⁄ 4  of the whole square and why they are equivalent even when they ‘don’t look the same,’ i.e. are noncongruent.

Task 3: Will this one work?

Students prove a special case of a square partitioned into noncongruent fourths..

Instructional Goals:

Common Core Content Standards

Reason with shapes and their attributes

2.G.A.3 Partition circles and rectangles into two, three, or four equal shares, describe the shares using the words halves, thirds, half of, a third of, etc., and describe the whole as two halves, three thirds, four fourths. Recognize that equal shares of identical wholes need not have the same shape.

3.G.A.2 Partition shapes into parts with equal areas. Express the area of each part as a unit fraction of the whole. For example, partition a shape into 4 parts with equal area, and describe the area of each part as 1⁄ 4  of the area of the shape.

Develop understanding of fractions as numbers.

3.NF.A.3 Explain equivalence of fractions in special cases, and compare fractions by reasoning about their size.

  1. Understand two fractions as equivalent (equal) if they are the same size, or the same point on a number line.

Standards for Mathematical Practice:

Practice Standard 3: Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.
  • Are both of these representations equivalent? Convince us.
  • Do you see any squares that are not partitioned into equal areas?
  • Would both these partitions (e.g. triangle 1⁄ 4  and rectangle 1⁄ 4) help solve a puzzle even though they are different shapes? Prove it.
  • Do you agree or disagree with what he said? why?
Practice Standard 5: Use appropriate tools strategically.
  • How can you use the tools available to prove that these two pieces are each ¼ of this whole square?
Practice Standard 6: Attend to precision.
  • What does it mean to say both of these shapes are 1⁄ 4  of the ‘whole’ square?
  • What does it mean to say both shapes are ‘equivalent?’

Fraction Schema:

Student misconceptions of fractions are frequently promoted and reinforced by static models of fractions, e.g. squares or circles partitioned in the same way where the total number of partitions marks the denominator and the ‘shaded’ partitions call out the numerator. This lesson engages students in constructing an understanding of unit fractions by creatively partitioning a unit square in several different ways and then constructing proofs of two key concepts for understanding fractions: 1. How noncongruent shapes can represent the same unit fraction, and 2. The importance of using the same unit whole when making comparisons.

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students express their thinking?

Task 1: How many ways can you partition the square?

  1. Paper Squares: Students fold, cut or draw lines to partition paper squares into fourths.
    1. Repeated Halving: Partition the squares in half horizontally, vertically, or diagonally and then partition that shape in half again.
  2. Draw on Grid Paper: Students create squares on grid paper and use the square units within the whole square to create fourths.
    1. Students who create a 2×2 square may struggle to partition their square beyond the ways shown in the game.
    2. A 4×4 square helps visualize other, more complex, partitions.
  3. Geoboard: Students use geo-bands to create a whole square and then partition it into fourths with trial and error, or based on the area of each partition.

Task 2: Will this one work?

  1. Measuring Area: Students use grid or dot paper and count the number of square units in each partition. They show that even though the ‘shapes’ are different, they are all 1⁄ 4  of the area of the whole square.
  2. Cut and Rearrange: Students cut out each of the partitions and rearrange them to show that the two squares have the same area as the two non-square rectangles.
    1. Some students may also cut each square or non-square rectangle in half, reorganize them so they are congruent to the other shape in the prompt.
  3. Move the Lines: On a geoboard or drawing, students will cut two of the congruent partitions in half by drawing a line or using a rubber band and remove the original partition to create four congruent areas. Proving why this works mathematically is very challenging and many students will struggle. Encourage students to use another strategy to try to prove it, e.g. the cut and rearrange strategy to help prove this.

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Inaccurate partitions, e.g. folds with edges that overlap or are not exact.
  • Miscalculate the area.
Misconceptions:
  • There are no more partitions after 4 parallel fourths, 2×2 square fourths, and two diagonals.
  • Fourths must all ‘look’ the same.
  • Fourths formed from different partitioning strategies are not equivalent.

Launch:

Pose the tasks

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles

Students identify all relevant visuals and understand what the puzzle is asking them to solve. Get a puzzle or two wrong and have students discuss why it did not work. Ask students to solve 1 or 2 puzzles by discussing which square will ‘work,’ i.e. is partitioned equally. Ask them if combining parts from two equal area squares (from different puzzles) would pull down the path evenly, i.e., two fourths from one puzzle and two from another.

Puzzle Analysis

3rd Grade/Fraction Concepts/Module 1/Equal Areas/Level 2

Puzzles in level 2 present four squares partitioned into halves, thirds, and fourths. Students must select the square that has been partitioned into equal areas. Comparing puzzles demonstrates that shapes can be partitioned equally in different ways (see above screenshots). Use puzzles with squares partitioned into four areas to launch an exploration into how many different ways can a whole square be cut into fourths and determine if noncongruent fourths are equivalent.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math puzzle?

  • What do you see in this puzzle? Ask students to pair share at least three things they notice.
  • What is JiJi asking us to figure out?
  • How did you figure out which square to choose?
  • How are the squares in these two puzzles partitioned differently? (See samples above.)
  • How are the partitions the same?
  • What would happen if we took two pieces from each of these squares and put them on the hooks ? (They would all pull down the path evenly because they are all 1⁄ 4  of the same square.)

What questions will help you uncover your students’ thinking?

  • Describe what you have done so far.
  • How can you convince me that your four partitions are equivalent, i.e. they are each 1⁄ 4  of the whole square?
  • How did you decide what tool to use and what to do 1st, 2nd, 3rd, etc.?

What questions will help advance student thinking?

  • Can you try a different strategy to find another way to partition the square (e.g. four equal areas)?
  • Would your strategy work if you used…(another tool)?
  • Are the partitions from this square equivalent to this other square? Why/why not?
  • What if we used two squares of different sizes? Would a fourth from a larger square be equivalent to a fourth of a smaller square? Why/why not?

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students construct a viable argument for why two noncongruent shapes may be equivalent and represent the same fractional part of a given whole.
  • Students use tools to create models that help prove why a square was partitioned equally.
  • Students explain that a unit fraction, 1⁄ b , can compose a ‘whole’ if iterated b times.
