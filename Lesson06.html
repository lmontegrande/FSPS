Estimate Fractions on a Number Line

 

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

In Estimate Fractions on a Number Line players are given a fraction to locate on a number line with only the whole numbers labeled. Players move a blast pad below the number line to the location of the given fraction. If correct, JiJi moves to the location of the fraction and the blast pad takes JiJi up off the screen. If the fraction location is incorrect on the number line, the blast pad moves off the screen without JiJi. The animation shows the interval from 0 to 1 being partitioned into the denominator number of equal parts. The animation then shows the unit fraction (one of these partitions) being iterated the numerator number of times to determine the correct location of that fraction on the number line.

Students will:

Create a number line and place several fractions on the number line. They will explain how they determined the placement of three of the fractions.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzle

Project puzzle from Level 2. Students mentally divide a 0 to 1 number line to analyze where to place the blast pad indicated by the fraction. Project a puzzles from Level 4. Students notice that the denominator defines the size of each of JiJi’s jumps and the numerator defines the number of jumps JiJi makes. Discuss benchmark and equivalent fractions as strategies for estimating where to place the fractions on the number line.

Task 2: Estimate placement of fractions on a number line

Have students work with a partner to create a number line and place given fractions on the number line. Use benchmark fractions, equivalent fractions and comparing fractions to help you place fractions on a number line. Work individually to draw out your number line and explain how you knew where to place three of the fractions.

Instructional Goals:

Common Core Content Standards

3.NF Develop understanding of fractions as numbers.

3.NF.A.3 Explain equivalence of fractions in special cases, and compare fractions by reasoning about their size.

3.NF.A.3a Understand two fractions as equivalent (equal) if they are the same size, or the same point on a number line.

3.NF.A.3b Recognize and generate simple equivalent fractions, e.g., 1⁄ 2  = 2⁄ 4, 4⁄ 6  = 2⁄ 3. Explain why the fractions are equivalent, e.g., by using a visual fraction model.

3.NF.A.3c Express whole numbers as fractions, and recognize fractions that are equivalent to whole numbers. Examples: Express 3 in the form 3 = 3⁄ 1; recognize that 6⁄ 1= 6; locate 4⁄ 4  and 1 at the same point of a number line diagram.

3.NF.A.3d Compare two fractions with the same numerator or the same denominator by reasoning about their size. Recognize that comparisons are valid only when the two fractions refer to the same whole. Record the results of comparisons with the symbols >, =, or <, and justify the conclusions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model.

4.NF Extend understanding of fraction equivalence and ordering.

4.NF.1 Explain why a fraction a⁄ b  is equivalent to a fraction (n × a)/(n × b) by using visual fraction models, with attention to how the number and size of the parts differ even though the two fractions themselves are the same size. Use this principle to recognize and generate equivalent fractions.

4.NF.2 Compare two fractions with different numerators and different denominators, e.g., by creating common denominators or numerators, or by comparing to a benchmark fraction such as 1⁄ 2. Recognize that comparisons are valid only when the two fractions refer to the same whole. Record the results of comparisons with symbols >, =, or < , and justify the conclusions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model.

4.NF.B.3.B Decompose a fraction into a sum of fractions with the same denominator in more than one way, recording each decomposition by an equation. Justify decompositions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model. Examples: 3⁄ 8  = 1⁄ 8  + 1⁄ 8  + 1⁄ 8  ; 3⁄ 8  = 1⁄ 8  + 2⁄ 8; 2 1⁄ 8  = 1 + 1 + 1⁄ 8  = 8⁄ 8  + 8⁄ 8  + 1⁄ 8.

5.NF Use equivalent fractions as a strategy to add and subtract fractions.

5.NF.1 Add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators (including mixed numbers) by replacing given fractions with equivalent fractions in such a way as to produce an equivalent sum or difference of fractions with like denominators. For example, 2⁄ 3  + 5⁄ 4  = 8⁄ 12  + 15⁄ 12  = 23⁄ 12 . (In general, a⁄ b  + c⁄ d  = (ad + bc)⁄ bd.)

5.NF.2 Use benchmark fractions and number sense of fractions to estimate mentally and assess the reasonableness of answers. For example, recognize an incorrect result 2⁄ 5  + 1⁄ 2  = 3⁄ 7, by observing that 3⁄ 7  < 1⁄ 2.

Standards for Mathematical Practice:

Practice Standard 2: Reason abstractly and quantitatively

  • How can you use benchmark fractions to help you compare fractions?
  • How can you use fraction equivalence to help you compare fractions?
  • Why did you place this fraction between this other fraction and 1?

Practice Standard 3: Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.

  • Can you find a fraction that is closer to one than this fraction? Convince me/us?
  • Explain how you can decompose a fraction to estimate where to place it on the number line.
  • Explain how you knew to put this fraction here on the number line.

Fraction Schema:

Comparing and ordering fractions and understanding of the relative magnitude of numbers develops when students place numbers on a number line and reason about their relative size. Creating and using a number line develops an understanding of order of numbers. Comparing two points on a number line the point to the left is less than the point to the right. Equivalent fractions occupy/name the same point on a number line. Using benchmark fractions, iteration of unit fractions and equivalence develops an understanding of fractions as numbers and reasonableness of answers in operations with fractions.

Materials

  • Problem
  • Fraction cards cut out
  • Straight edge
  • Manipulatives (to help students accurately partition the number line into 12, 14, etc.)

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students approach the task?

Task 1: ST Math Estimate Fractions on a Number Line puzzles

Students discuss with a partner where they would place a given fraction on the number line. They share their thinking with the whole group. Strategies will be similar to strategies listed under task 2.

Task 2: Estimate placement of fractions on a number line

  1. Benchmark fractions: Students use benchmark fractions of half and one to help them place fractions on the number line.
  2. Equivalent fractions: Students use a more familiar equivalent fraction to place a fraction on the number line (e.g., 6⁄ 8  = 3⁄ 4). Students also recognize fractions that are equivalent to whole numbers. (e.g., 3⁄ 1, 4⁄ 2)
  3. Comparing fractions: Students compare the size of fractions to determine which goes first on the number line of which is closer to one. (e.g., 11⁄ 12  is only 1⁄ 12  away from one so it is closer than 7⁄ 8  because it is smaller than 1⁄ 8)

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Students miscount markings.
  • Students are not precise with laying the fractions at accurate distances from other fractions and marking their number line.
Misconceptions:
  • Students begin their counting at the 0 hash mark.
  • Students do not consider the distance from 0 to 1 before beginning a count (e.g., 3 is half of 5 because there are two numbers before it (1 and 2) and two numbers after it (4 and 5) so 3 is in the middle).
  • Students do not attend to the relative distances between fractions on the number line.
  • Students pay attention to and count the number of hash marks and not the distance between hash marks.

Launch:

Pose the tasks

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles

Students identify all relevant visuals and understand what the puzzle is asking them to solve. Get a puzzle or two wrong and have students discuss why it did not work.

Puzzle Analysis

4th Grade/Mixed Numbers/ Mixed Numbers on a Number Line/Estimate Fractions on a Number Line/ Levels 2 and 4.

Level 2: JiJi jumps to the location of the fraction on the number line and the blast pad takes JiJi up. The fraction animates to show the size of each jump (denominator) and the number of jumps (numerator) JiJi will take. Number line is between 0 and 1. Discuss how students determine where to place fractions on the number line. Look for students to describe fraction locations in relationship to benchmark fractions.

Level 4: These puzzles include fractions with numerators denominators. Look for a puzzle equivalent to a whole number. Discuss the equivalence and the relationship of the numerator and denominator. Continue discussion of how to best determine fraction location on the number line, using benchmark fractions and equivalent fractions.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math Puzzle?

  • What do you notice in this puzzle? Ask students to pair share at least 3 things they notice.
  • How does the animation show that the size of each jump indicates the denominator and the number of jumps JiJi will take indicates the numerator?
  • What fractions are easy for you to visualize on the number line between 0 and 1? Between 2 and 3?
  • What do we know about the distance from 0 to 1⁄ 4  and the distance between 1 and 1 1⁄ 4?
  • Name a fraction that is closer to 1 than this fraction.

Task 2: Estimate placement of fractions on a number line

  1. Pose the task: Partners create a number line using all of the fractions on the cards (see attachment). Be as accurate as possible.
  2. Individuals: Record your number line (be as accurate as possible). Select three of the fractions (challenge yourself) you placed on the number line and explain how you and your partner determined where to place these fractions on the number line.

Encourage students to think of a plan to solve the mathematical task.

  • What strategy will help you solve this task? (SMP 1, 4)
  • How will you use the relationship of the fractions to help you accurately place the numbers on the number line? (SMP 2)
  • How will you record your thinking? (SMP 3,4,6)
  • How will you evaluate your solution? (SMP 1)

Explore:

Monitor as the students generate solutions and strategies.

Teacher’s Role:

  • Use questioning strategies to help stuck students or advance early finishers.
  • Select and sequence students’ strategies which align with the instructional goals.
  • Generate initial and follow up questions aligned to the goals and the anticipated solutions and solution strategies.

Student’s Role:

  • Choose a solution strategy and math tools which will help them solve the task.
  • Demonstrate the Standards of Mathematical Practice listed in the Goals section.

What questions will help students focus on the problem?

  • What does a number line need in order to be accurate?
  • What strategy can you use to compare these two fractions and then place them accurately on the number line?
  • Where is 1 ( 1⁄ 2, 2, 0, 1 1⁄ 2) on your number line? How can this help you place other numbers on your number line?
  • Are there any fractions you know that can help you determine where to put this fraction (e.g., 25⁄ 12) on the number line?

What questions will help you uncover your students’ thinking?

  • Describe what you have done so far?
  • How can you convince me that this fraction should be placed here on the number line?
  • Does the distance between 0 and 1⁄ 4  have to be the same length as the distance between 1 1⁄ 2  and 1 3⁄ 4? Why?
  • How did you decide to divide your number line?
  • Did you use benchmark fractions to help you place any of these fractions? Explain.
  • How did you determine where to place 1 1⁄ 2?

What questions will help advance student thinking?

  • Could you decompose these two fractions into unit fractions to determine where to place them on the number line?
  • Is there a different strategy you could use to place this fraction on your number line?
  • How does the distance between these two fractions compare to the distance between these two fractions? Why?
  • What other fractions would be easy to show on your number line using your bar model?
  • How many twelfths are in your entire number line?

Select and Sequence:

What student strategies will support the lesson’s math goals?

The sequence below is one example. You’re students may not have used all the strategies described below. Your students will vary in the language they use.

Misconceptions (ask students before the share out if they feel comfortable sharing their misconception; misconceptions may be anonymous)

  1. Share a number line with misconceptions in ordering some of the fractions and have students identify the misconceptions.
  2. Share a number line with misconceptions in distance relationships of fractions and have students identify the misconceptions.
  3. Have several students share how they placed a fraction on their number line.
    1. “I started with a number line that went just past 2 because I knew that 25⁄ 12  is 1⁄ 12  greater than 2 and I knew that 11⁄ 12  was 1⁄ 12  less than one so I put it the same distance to the left of 1 as the distance from 2 to 25⁄ 12.”
    2. “I knew that 3⁄ 6  is equal to 1⁄ 2  so I put it half way between 0 and 1. Then I knew that 3⁄ 4  is halfway between 1⁄ 2  and 1.”
    3. “I knew that 14⁄ 8  was 2⁄ 8  or 1⁄ 4  less than 2 which is equivalent to 16⁄ 8.”
    4. “I knew that 11⁄ 12  is only 1⁄ 12  less than 1 and 7⁄ 8  is 1⁄ 8  less than 1, so 11⁄ 12  is closer to 1.”

Summary:

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher’s Role:

Highlight students’ strategies and reasoning that connect to the mathematics in the task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and discuss ways to compare representations. Record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the Using Benchmark and Equivalent Fractions to Estimate Location on a Number Line that support learning goals

  • How does this strategy compare to this strategy? Your strategy?
  • Why can both strategies be used to place the same number on the number line?
  • Which strategy is more efficient? Why?
  • Why did you use the benchmark 1 to help you determine where to place these two fractions?
  • You knew that 6⁄ 3  is greater than 3⁄ 6. What else do you need to know in order to place them on your number line? Explain.
  • How does the distance between these two numbers compare?
  • Why is it important to pay attention to the spaces between numbers on a number line?
  • What is another name for this point? (1 1⁄ 2  or 3⁄ 2)
  • How is counting the unit fractions like counting whole numbers?

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students accurately describe how they placed the different numbers on their number line.
  • Students understand how to use benchmark fractions to determine where to place a number on their number line.
  • Students understand how to use the relationship between two fractions to place numbers on a number line. (Look for students using benchmark fractions, equivalence and comparing fractions. Some examples are listed in the Select and Sequence.)
  • Students understand that equivalent fractions share the same point on a number line.
  • Students understand that when they divide a number line into halves each iterated half is one more than the one to the left of it (i.e., 1⁄2, 2⁄2, 3⁄2).
  • Students understand that one-half to the right of 1 is 11⁄2  and one-half to the right of 2 is 21⁄2, etc..
  • Students understand that two different number lines do not need to have the same distance between 0 and 1. However, on one number line the distance from 0 to 1 must be the same as the distance from 5 to 6 and the distance between 0 and ¼ is one-fourth of the distance between 0 and 1, etc.

Student’s Role:

  • Use the Standards of Mathematical Practice listed in the Goals section.
  • Share their mathematical thinking with the class.
  • As your classmates share their thinking, you need to think about:
    • What is your level of understanding of your classmate’s strategy?
    • What questions can you ask to clarify your own understanding of the strategy?
    • How does your classmates’ strategies compare to your strategies?
    • Would you change any of your work? Why?
    • How are the strategies similar and how are they different?
    • Do you agree or disagree with your classmate’s strategy? Why or why not?
