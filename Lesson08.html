Playground Areas

 

Lesson Overview

ST Math Game:

Game:

In Bricks, players need to place one or more colored shapes into the area of an ‘empty’ shape in the center of the puzzle. When players click and place pieces that fill the shape exactly, JiJi will cross the screen. If the shapes do not fit exactly, the overlapping sections drop and block JiJi’s path. All the shapes have a proportional relationship to each other. They have either equivalent areas but are not congruent shapes or are a fraction (½ or ¼) of the area of all the other shapes.

Students will:

Determine the fractional area of each shape within a rectangle that is partitioned into multiple areas.

Teachers will:

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzles

Project puzzles from Level 2 of Bricks. Students describe and use models to explain how a shape can be partitioned to create two shapes with the same area (12) of the whole shape. Students discuss and prove that two non-congruent shapes can be the same size.

Task 2: Playground Areas

Project a large rectangle partitioned into multiple areas to determine the fractional area of each shape within the rectangle. Have various students explain how they determined what fraction of the whole different sections cover.

Instructional Goals:

Common Core Content Standards

Develop understanding of fractions as numbers.

3.NF.A.1 Understand a fraction 1/b as the quantity formed by 1 part when a whole is partitioned into b equal parts; understand a fraction a/b as the quantity formed by a parts of size 1/b.

Build fractions from unit fractions by applying and extending previous understandings of operations on whole numbers.

4.NF.B.4 Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication to multiply a fraction by a whole number.

4.NF.B.4b Understand a multiple of a/b as a multiple of 1/b, and use this understanding to multiply a fraction by a whole number. For example, use a visual fraction model to express 3 × (⅖) as 6 × (⅕), recognizing this product as 6/5. (In general, n × (a/b) = (n × a)/b.)

4.NF.B.3b Decompose a fraction into a sum of fractions with the same denominator in more than one way, recording each decomposition by an equation. Justify decompositions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model. Examples: ⅜ = ⅛ + ⅛ + ⅛ ; ⅜ = ⅛ + 2/8 ; 2 ⅛ = 1 + 1 + ⅛ = 8/8 + 8/8 + ⅛.

Use equivalent fractions as a strategy to add and subtract fractions.

5.NF.A.1 Add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators (including mixed numbers) by replacing given fractions with equivalent fractions in such a way as to produce an equivalent sum or difference of fractions with like denominators. For example, ⅔ + 5/4 = 8/12 + 15/12 = 23/12. (In general, a/b + c/d = (ad + bc)/bd.)

Standards for Mathematical Practice:

Practice Standard 2: Reason Quantitatively and Abstractly.

  • Do you notice any relationships among the size of the different areas? How can they help you figure out some of these values?
  • How did you determine the size of these areas?
  • How can you use benchmark fractions to help you determine the size of these areas?
  • How can you use fraction equivalence to help you determine the size of these areas?

Practice Standard 3: Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others. of others.

  • How does this help you justify your reasoning?
  • Explain how you knew the size of these areas.
  • What should be the sum of all of these values? Why?

Fraction Schema:

Partitioning a shape into proportional sections and comparing the sizes of the partitions develops an understanding of the relative size of fractions (e.g., ½ of ¼ is ⅛). Actively manipulating the partitions within a shape provides students with an understanding of the relative size of partitions and proof that two non-congruent shapes can have the same area and have the same fractional relationship to the whole shape. Finding the size of a fractional part of a fractional part leads to understanding multiplication of a fraction by a fraction. Iterating fractional parts to find the whole involves going from the part to the whole or multiplicative reasoning (reasoning up).

Anticipated Student Responses: How might students express their thinking?

Task 2: Playground Areas

  1. Cut and try: Students may cut out all of the pieces then iterate lotsplayground areas to find their fractional value.
  2. Repeated Halving: Students may start with half of the whole and continue to divide sections into half to determine the smaller size sections. This will work for all sections.
  3. Reason Proportionally: Students may visually combine sections to determine the size of some sections (i.e. the two small right triangles are half of the small square which is half of the whole playground, so one of the triangles is 14of the small square and 18 of the whole playground).

What possible errors or misconceptions might students show?

Errors:
  • Students make computational errors.
  • Students think half of the playground is the whole.
Misconceptions:
  • Students do not attend to the relative sizes of the playground areas in the playground.
  • Students count each section as its fractional size of the next larger shape it is part of. (e.g., C is ½ because it is half of the left side).

Launch:

Pose the tasks

Task 1: Present ST Math puzzle

Students identify all relevant visuals and understand what the puzzle is asking them to solve. Get a puzzle or two wrong and have students discuss why it did not work.

Puzzle Analysis

5th Grade/Area/Equal Areas and Bricks/Level 2; Equal Areas/Level 3 Bricks.

Bricks:

Level 2: Students select shapes from 4 choices to fit into the outline of a shape, without overlapping or leaving gaps. Shapes must be oriented correctly to fit. Students discuss the relative size of all of the shapes. The triangles are one-half the area of the square. The parallelograms are the same size as the square. The triangles are therefore one-half the size of the parallelograms.

What questions will help students focus on the math in the ST Math puzzle?

  • What do you see in this puzzle?
  • What fraction of the whole is each of these sections?
  • What do you notice about how this shape can be partitioned into two equal sized sections?
  • Drag the parallelogram to the square and return. Replay the action, pause and ask students to prove that the square and parallelogram are the same size.

Task 2: Playground Areas (worksheet attached)

Students determine the area of each of the lots in the Playground (the whole). Three different Playground Areas are included. The easier task may be used first to scaffold the learning or in place of the more complex Playground Areas for lower grade levels.

Encourage students to think of a plan to solve the mathematical task.

  • What strategy will help you solve this task? (SMP 1, 4)
  • What do you know (size of which lot do you know)? How can that help you figure out the size of a lot you do not know? (SMP 7)
  • How will you record your thinking and share it with the class? (SMP 3,4,6)
  • How will you evaluate your solution? or How can you check to make sure this is correct? (SMP 1) 

Explore:

Monitor as the students generate solutions and strategies.

Teacher’s Role:

  • Monitor as the students generate solutions and solution strategies. Select and sequence students’ strategies which align with the instructional goals.
  • Generate initial and follow up questions aligned to the goals and the anticipated solutions and solution strategies.

Student’s Role:

  • Choose a solution strategy and math tools which will help them solve the task.
  • Demonstrate the Standards of Mathematical Practice listed in the Goals section. (e.g., Students find the total of the playground to be 1 and explain that all of the sizes of the playground areas in the whole playground should add to 1 because that is the unit whole. During whole class discussion students will construct an argument for the fraction one of the playground areas is of the whole playground.).

What questions will help students focus on the problem?

  • What do you notice about the relationship between these two sections?
  • What strategy can you use to help you compare this section to the whole playground?
  • How many playground areas of this size would it take to make a whole playground?

What questions will help you uncover your students’ thinking?

  • Describe what you have done so far?
  • How can you convince me that the area of this section is ¼ of the whole playground?
  • Does a section that is ¼ the area of the whole city have to be the same shape as another section that is ¼ the area of the whole city? Justify your reasoning.
  • How did you determine the size of this section?

What questions will help advance student thinking?

  • Could you decompose all of these playground areas into sums of the same unit fraction?
  • How would you represent these playground areas’ sizes with equations if you decomposed them into sums of the same unit fraction (e.g., sixteenths)? What is another equation you could use (multiplication)?
  • Is there a different strategy you could have used to determine the size of this playground area?
  • If you add up the size of all of the playground areas what will the total be? Justify your reasoning and then calculate the total.
  • How will this help you know if you have a correct solution?
  • What equation would you use to express how you found the size of this playground area? (Use questions similar to this one for students who have easily solved the problem or students you feel can benefit from this challenge.)

Select and Sequence:

What student strategies will support the lesson’s math goals?

The sequence below is one example. You’re students may not have used all the strategies described below. Your students will vary in the language they use.

What sequence of strategies do you intend to use?

  1. Share two or three students’ thinking in different ways to find the size of one of the playground areas. The following is a possible sequence of sharing:
    1. Students cut out all of the playground areas and find out how many it takes to fill the whole city. This shows iterating a unit fraction. Help the student represent this thinking with an equation.
    2. Students fold or cut out the size of the whole city to find the size of the playground areas.
    3. Students reason about the size of the playground areas in their heads and compare different playground area sizes by halving. Discuss with the student how they can represent this thinking with an equation.
    4. Students represent the relationship of the playground areas with equations.
  2. Share a misconception such as the total for all of their playground areas does not add up to one or how the taxes were determined incorrectly. Students identify the misconceptions.
  3. Have several students share how they determined the size of the playground areas.
    1. “I started with the A and B lots and knew they were 14 the area of the whole city . Then I knew that A was half of that so it was 18 the area of the whole city. B was the same size.”
    2. “I knew that E, F and G were ¼ the area of the whole city. F was half of that so it was ⅛ the area of the whole city. E and G were both half the size of F so they were ½ of ⅛ which is 1/16.”

Summary:

Whole Class Discussion

Teacher’s Role:

Highlight the students’ strategies and reasoning and connect them to the mathematics in both the ST Math puzzle and the mathematical task.

How will you promote students’ strategies, representations and thinking?

Use student work and compare representations, record on chart paper or use a document camera to show student work.

Questions for the "Playground Areas" that support learning goals.

  • How do these strategies compare? Compare it to your strategy.
  • Why can both strategies be used to determine the area of the sections?
  • Which strategy is more efficient? Why?
  • What size is this playground area? How did you determine the size of this playground area? How many of this size playground areas would it take to make one whole playground?
  • How can we use an equation to represent your thinking?
  • What was the first area you figured out? Why did you choose to start with that playground area?
  • How did knowing the size of this area help you determine the size of other areas?
  • You knew that area C was smaller than area M. Was that enough to help you determine the size of area C? Why or why not?
  • What is the relationship of the playground area of G to area of F?
  • When you added up the fractional area of all of the playground areas, what was the total? How does this relate to the whole city?

Identify what you want students to take away from the discussion.

  • Students accurately describe how they determined the size of each playground area.
  • Students understand that a playground area sized 1/b is one part when the whole playground is partitioned into b equal parts.
  • Students understand that it takes b playground areas of size 1/b to make the whole playground.
  • Students explain and justify how they use halving to help them determine the size of the playground areas.
  • Students describe why areas that are the same fractional size of a whole do not have to be the same shape (non-congruent).
  • Students understand that when they add up the fractional size of all parts of a whole area, they will get 1. (This is a very important concept that may reveal gaps in students' schemas.)
  • Students understand that ½ of ½ is ¼ and ½ of ¼ is ⅛? If appropriate ask students to writing an equation to represent this concept.

Student’s Role:

  • Use the Standards of Mathematical Practice listed in the Goals section.
  • Share their mathematical thinking with the class.
  • As your classmates share their thinking, you need to think about:
    • What is your level of understanding of your classmate’s strategy?
    • What questions can you ask to clarify your own understanding of the strategy?
    • How does your classmate's strategies compare to your strategies?
    • Would you change any of your work? Why?
    • How are these two strategies similar and how are they different?
    • Do you agree or disagree with your classmate’s strategy? Why or why not?
